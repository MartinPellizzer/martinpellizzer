Eulophidium tainioides

Eulophidium tainioides, commonly known as the golden nematode, is a microscopic parasitic nematode that primarily infests the roots of cruciferous plants such as cabbage, broccoli, and cauliflower.

This plant-parasitic nematode is known for its ability to cause significant damage to agricultural crops by feeding on root tissues, leading to stunted growth and reduced yield. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is particularly problematic in regions with warm climates. The life cycle of Eulophidium tainioides involves several stages, including egg, four juvenile stages, and an adult stage, all of which contribute to its persistence in the soil.

Due to its destructive nature, managing Eulophidium tainioides often requires crop rotation, soil fumigation, or the use of resistant plant varieties.
