Aegiphila scandens

Aegiphila scandens, commonly known as the climbing aegiphila, is a shrub native to the Caribbean and parts of South America.

It is characterized by its slender, twining stems that allow it to climb and spread across other vegetation. The plant has small, opposite leaves that are often hairy and have a rough texture. Its flowers are typically white or pale pink, arranged in clusters, and bloom during the warmer months.

Aegiphila scandens is often found in moist, shaded areas such as forest understories and along stream banks.

Morphology

Aegiphila scandens has compound leaves that are opposite in arrangement, with ovate to elliptic leaflets that are usually glossy green and vary in size from 2 to 5 centimeters.

The stems are slender, somewhat woody, with a smooth, light gray bark that grows in a climbing or trailing pattern. The flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, with white to pale pink petals and yellow stamens, blooming in spring and summer, and are pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a dry, capsule that releases small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant and absorbing moisture from the soil.
