Aechmea laevigata

Aechmea laevigata, commonly known as the silver pine bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped inflorescence, which consists of long, narrow leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant's leaves are covered in a dense layer of fine, silver-white hairs, giving it its common name. It produces striking red or orange flowers that emerge from the center of the rosette, often followed by small, violet-colored fruits.

Aechmea laevigata thrives in humid, shaded environments and is often used as an ornamental plant in tropical gardens.

Morphology

Aechmea laevigata has rosette-shaped leaves that are narrow, strap-like, and arranged in a spiral around the central stem, with a waxy, dark green color and a leathery texture.

The stem is short and stout, with a smooth, pale green bark that grows upright from the base of the leaf rosette. The plant produces terminal inflorescences with clustered, bell-shaped flowers that are typically pink or red, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by insects such as bees and hummingbirds. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind and water.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the surrounding soil.
