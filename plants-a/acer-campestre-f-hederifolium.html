Acer campestre f. hederifolium

Acer campestre f. hederifolium, commonly known as the creeping maple, is a compact, low-growing shrub native to Europe.

It features dark green, heart-shaped leaves with a smooth texture and a slightly waxy surface. The plant produces small, red-orange flowers in early spring, followed by winged seeds that are dispersed by the wind. It thrives in well-drained soil and partial shade, making it suitable for rock gardens or as a ground cover.

Its ornamental value comes from its attractive foliage and ability to form dense, mat-like growth.

Native Distribution

Acer campestre f. hederifolium is primarily native to central and eastern Europe, including countries such as Germany, France, Italy, and the Czech Republic.

This variety is commonly found in woodlands, hedgerows, and along riverbanks. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade. The plant is known for its distinctive heart-shaped leaves and is often used in horticulture for its ornamental value.

It is a popular choice for landscaping due to its hardiness and adaptability to various environmental conditions.

Introduced Distribution

Acer campestre f. hederifolium was mainly introduced to Europe during the 18th and 19th centuries as an ornamental plant.

It was valued for its attractive, ivy-like leaves and its ability to thrive in a variety of soil conditions. This cultivar is commonly found in gardens and parks, particularly in temperate regions. Its introduction contributed to the diversity of maple species cultivated in horticulture.

However, it is not native to Europe and has been primarily used for decorative purposes rather than ecological restoration.
