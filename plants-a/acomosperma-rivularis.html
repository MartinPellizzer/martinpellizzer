Acomosperma rivularis

Acomosperma rivularis, commonly known as the river sedge, is a flowering plant species native to parts of Southeast Asia.

It is a herbaceous perennial that typically grows in wet or marshy environments. The plant features narrow, grass-like leaves and produces small, clustered flowers that are often green or brown in color. It has a fibrous root system that helps stabilize soil in riparian zones.

Acomosperma rivularis plays an important ecological role in maintaining wetland ecosystems.

Morphology

Acomosperma rivularis has elongated, narrow leaves that are arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green color and a slightly waxy texture.

The stem is slender, with a smooth, light brown bark that exhibits a climbing growth pattern. Its small, inconspicuous flowers are clustered in axillary clusters, featuring yellowish petals and a short blooming period during the spring; they are pollinated by wind. The fruit is a small capsule that splits open to release seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and anchor the plant to rocky or sandy substrates, aiding in its survival in harsh environments.
