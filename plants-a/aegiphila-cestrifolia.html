Aegiphila cestrifolia

Aegiphila cestrifolia, commonly known as the woolly false holly, is a flowering plant native to the Caribbean and parts of Central and South America.

It is characterized by its compact, shrub-like growth habit and its distinctive, woolly, silver-gray foliage. The plant produces small, clustered white or pale yellow flowers that bloom in the spring and summer months. It thrives in well-drained, sandy soils and is often found in coastal or limestone habitats.

Aegiphila cestrifolia is valued for its ornamental appeal and is sometimes cultivated in gardens for its unique appearance.

Morphology

Aegiphila cestrifolia has narrow, lance-shaped leaves that are arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is slender, woody, and covered with a rough, reddish-brown bark that becomes smoother with age. Its small, clustered flowers are pink to reddish in color, with five petals and a tubular throat, blooming in late spring to early summer and pollinated by insects.
