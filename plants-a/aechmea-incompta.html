Aechmea incompta

Aechmea incompta, commonly known as the velvet bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients from the environment. The leaves are typically dark green with a velvety texture, and they are covered in a fine layer of trichomes that aid in water absorption. The plant produces vibrant, showy inflorescences that range in color from red to orange, often appearing in the spring or summer.

Aechmea incompta is well-suited for indoor cultivation due to its adaptability and low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea incompta has leaves that are broad, strap-like, and arranged in a rosette, with a waxy, silver-green color and a leathery texture.

The plant has a short, unbranched stem that is covered in a dense, fibrous, grayish-brown bark. Its flowers are borne in a spike-like inflorescence, consisting of pale pink to white bracts with yellowish anthers, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by wind and insects. The fruit is a small, three-lobed berry that disperses its seeds through animal consumption and wind.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring in the soil, with some rhizomes contributing to the plant's clonal growth.
