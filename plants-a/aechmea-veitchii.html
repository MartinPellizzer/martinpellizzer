Aechmea veitchii

Aechmea veitchii, commonly known as the Veitch's Bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant features vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences that can range in color from red to orange, adding ornamental value. Its foliage is typically dark green with a waxy texture, and it thrives in bright, indirect light with high humidity.

Aechmea veitchii is popular as a houseplant due to its aesthetic appeal and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea veitchii has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, stiff, and covered in a dense layer of trichomes, giving them a silvery-blue appearance.

The leaves are arranged in a tight spiral around the central rosette, with each leaf reaching up to 30 cm in length. The plant has a short, unbranched stem that is covered in a thick, fibrous, greenish-brown bark. It produces inflorescences with violet to purple flowers that bloom in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects such as bees and flies.

The fruit is a capsule that contains numerous small, dark brown seeds, which are dispersed by wind and water.
