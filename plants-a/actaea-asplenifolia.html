Actaea asplenifolia

Actaea asplenifolia, commonly known as the fernleaf bugbane, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to eastern North America.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its finely divided, fern-like leaves that give it its common name. The plant produces clusters of white to pale pink flowers in late summer, which attract pollinators such as bees and butterflies. Actaea asplenifolia typically grows in moist, shaded woodlands and thrives in rich, well-drained soil.

Its foliage and flowers contribute to the ecological diversity of forest understory environments.

Morphology

Actaea asplenifolia has compound, pinnately arranged leaves that are ovate to elliptic in shape, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes reddish underside.

The leaves are typically 5-15 cm long and have a finely toothed margin. The stems are slender, smooth, and green, growing upright with a somewhat hairy texture when young. The plant produces small, greenish-white flowers in upright clusters during late spring to early summer, relying on wind for pollination.

The fruit is a red, fleshy berry containing small seeds, which are dispersed by birds and mammals.
