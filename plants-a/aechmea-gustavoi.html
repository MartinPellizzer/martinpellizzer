Aechmea gustavoi

Aechmea gustavoi, commonly known as the Gustavo's bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of Central and South America.

It features a rosette of broad, dark green leaves that are often covered in a dense layer of trichomes, giving them a fuzzy appearance. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences that range in color from red to orange, often with a striking contrast against the foliage. Its unique structure allows it to collect and store water in the central rosette, adapting to its humid, shaded habitat.

Aechmea gustavoi is valued in horticulture for its ornamental appeal and ability to thrive in indoor environments with proper care.

Morphology

Aechmea gustavoi has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, stiff, and often have a waxy texture, arranged in a tight spiral at the base of the plant.

The leaves are typically dark green with a bluish tinge and can grow up to 30 cm in length. The plant has a short, underground stem with a fibrous, light-colored root system that helps anchor it in the soil. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a spathe, with small, clustered white flowers that bloom during the rainy season and are pollinated by insects.

The fruit is a capsule that contains numerous small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind and water.
