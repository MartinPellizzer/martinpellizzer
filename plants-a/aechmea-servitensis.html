Aechmea servitensis

Aechmea servitensis, commonly known as the velvet bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaf structure, which forms a central cup that collects water and nutrients. The leaves are thick, fleshy, and covered with a dense layer of trichomes, giving them a velvety texture. The plant produces striking inflorescences with colorful flowers that bloom in shades of red, orange, and yellow.

It thrives in humid, shaded environments and is often cultivated as an ornamental plant for its aesthetic appeal.

Morphology

Aechmea servitensis has leaves that are strap-shaped, arranged in a rosette, and can grow up to 40 cm in length, with a waxy, bluish-green color and a pointed tip.

The plant has a short, stout stem with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that remains hidden beneath the leaves. Its flowers are clustered in a dense inflorescence, with white petals and bright yellow stamens, blooming during the dry season and pollinated primarily by birds. The fruit is a bright red, fleshy capsule that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in the soil, with some rhizomes extending to form a network.
