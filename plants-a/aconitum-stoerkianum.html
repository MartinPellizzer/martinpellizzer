Aconitum stoerkianum

Aconitum stoerkianum, commonly known as Stoerk's monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking blue to purple hooded flowers that resemble a medieval monk's habit. The plant typically grows to a height of 1 to 2 meters, with palmate leaves that are deeply lobed and have a distinctive appearance. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade, often found in alpine meadows and rocky slopes.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it is important to handle the plant with care and avoid ingestion.

Morphology

Aconitum stoerkianum has large, compound leaves that are palmately arranged, with lanceolate to ovate leaflets that are dark green on top and lighter underneath.

The stem is upright, hairy, and has a rough, dark brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are showy, bell-shaped, and typically blue or purple, blooming in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a capsule that releases small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, helping the plant survive in its alpine or subalpine habitat.
