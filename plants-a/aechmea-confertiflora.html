Aechmea confertiflora

Aechmea confertiflora, commonly known as the dense-flowered bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients from rain and debris. The plant produces vibrant, showy inflorescences with flowers that range in color from red to orange, attracting pollinators such as bees and hummingbirds. Its foliage is often adorned with white or pale green markings, enhancing its ornamental appeal.

Aechmea confertiflora thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular in horticulture for its aesthetic value and ease of care.

Morphology

Aechmea confertiflora has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, arranged in a compact circle, with a waxy green surface and a bluish-gray underside.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, with the leaves forming a dense rosette that holds water, and the base of the rosette is covered in a thick, fibrous mat. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, with small, greenish-white flowers that bloom during the rainy season and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a berry that turns red when mature, dispersing its seeds through animals that eat the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in the soil, with some rhizomes extending from the base of the rosette.
