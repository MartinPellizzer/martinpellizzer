Aechmea patriciae

Aechmea patriciae, commonly known as the Patricia's bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of Central America.

It features a rosette of narrow, grass-like leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater to support its growth. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences with flowers in shades of red, orange, and yellow. Its foliage is often adorned with intricate patterns and a waxy coating, enhancing its ornamental appeal.

Aechmea patriciae thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular as a houseplant due to its low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea patriciae has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, sword-like, and arranged in a dense spiral, with a waxy, dark green surface that often has a silvery or bluish tint.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, fibrous caudex that is covered in a thick, spongy, and sometimes reddish-brown bark. Its inflorescence consists of a branched spike bearing small, tubular flowers that range from white to pale pink, blooming during the dry season and pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a bright red, fleshy capsule that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in rocky or well-drained soil.
