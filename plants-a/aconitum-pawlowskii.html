Aconitum pawlowskii

Aconitum pawlowskii, commonly known as the Siberian monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking blue to purple spurred flowers that bloom in summer. The plant has dark green, palmately compound leaves that are deeply lobed and often covered in a waxy coating. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade, making it a popular choice for woodland gardens.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it should be handled with care and is not recommended for use in edible gardens.

Morphology

Aconitum pawlowskii has compound, pinnately arranged leaves that are large, deeply lobed, and typically green with a slightly hairy surface.

The stem is tall and upright, with a smooth, dark brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, arranged in dense spikes, and are pollinated primarily by insects such as bees. The fruit is a follicle that releases small, flat seeds through wind dispersal.

The roots are tuberous, thick, and fibrous, adapted for storage and anchoring the plant in moist, shaded environments.
