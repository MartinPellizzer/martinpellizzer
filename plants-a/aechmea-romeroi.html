Aechmea romeroi

Aechmea romeroi, commonly known as the bromeliad, is a flowering plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, known as a rosette, which collects rainwater. The plant produces striking inflorescences with colorful bracts that can last for several months. Aechmea romeroi thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

It is often cultivated as an ornamental plant due to its aesthetic appeal and low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea romeroi has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, sword-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a waxy surface and a deep green color.

The plant has a short, thick stem covered in a fibrous, brownish bark, and it grows upright with a compact form. Its inflorescence is a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, with small, white to pink flowers that bloom during the summer months and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a berry-like structure that turns red when mature, dispersing seeds through birds and water.

The roots are fibrous and adapted to absorb moisture from the surrounding environment.
