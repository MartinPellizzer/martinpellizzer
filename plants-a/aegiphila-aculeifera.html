Aegiphila aculeifera

Aegiphila aculeifera, commonly known as the spiny aegiphila, is a shrub native to the Caribbean, particularly found in Cuba and the Bahamas.

It is characterized by its aromatic, lance-shaped leaves that are often covered in fine hairs. The plant produces small, yellowish flowers arranged in clusters, which bloom during the dry season. Its stems are woody and may bear small spines, giving it a distinctive appearance.

Aegiphila aculeifera is adapted to coastal and limestone soils, making it a hardy plant in tropical and subtropical environments.

Morphology

Aegiphila aculeifera has leaves that are opposite in arrangement, ovate in shape, and typically range from 2 to 5 centimeters in length, with a dark green color and a slightly hairy texture.

The stem is erect, cylindrical, and has a rough, fissured bark that is often reddish-brown in color. Its flowers are small, clustered in inflorescences, with white petals and yellow stamens, blooming from late spring to early summer, and are pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that releases small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are shallow and fibrous, adapted to absorb moisture efficiently from the dry, sandy soils it inhabits.
