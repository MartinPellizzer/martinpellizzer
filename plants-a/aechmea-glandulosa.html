Aechmea glandulosa

Aechmea glandulosa, commonly known as the velvet bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaf structure, which forms a central cup to collect rainwater. The leaves are typically broad, fleshy, and covered in a dense layer of fine, hair-like trichomes that give the plant its velvety appearance. The plant produces striking inflorescences with vibrant flowers that bloom in shades of red, orange, and yellow.

It thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular as a decorative houseplant due to its unique appearance and low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea glandulosa has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, fleshy, and sword-like, arranged in a dense spiral at the base, with a dark green to blue-gray color and a waxy texture.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, fibrous caudex that is often covered in a dense layer of trichomes, giving it a rough, hairy appearance. Its inflorescence is a tall, erect spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, with small, clustered flowers that are typically white or pale pink, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by insects such as bees and flies. The fruit is a red, fleshy berry that contains numerous small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by birds and mammals that eat the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for water storage, helping the plant survive in its tropical, often arid habitat.
