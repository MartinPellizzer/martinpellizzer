Aegiphila paludosa

Aegiphila paludosa, commonly known as marsh aegiphila, is a herbaceous plant native to wetland environments in South America.

It typically grows in moist, sandy soils and is often found in coastal areas or along riverbanks. The plant has slender, upright stems and small, opposite leaves that are often covered in fine hairs. Its flowers are clustered in dense, terminal inflorescences and are usually pink or purple in color.

Aegiphila paludosa plays a role in stabilizing soil in its native habitat and provides habitat for various invertebrates.

Morphology

Aegiphila paludosa has leaves that are typically ovate to lanceolate in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, with a moderate size and a dark green color.

The stem is slender, covered with a smooth, light green bark, and exhibits a creeping growth pattern. Its flowers are small, clustered in umbels, with white petals and yellow stamens, blooming during the spring season, and are pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that releases tiny seeds through wind dispersal.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, allowing the plant to anchor itself and absorb nutrients from the wet, swampy habitat.
