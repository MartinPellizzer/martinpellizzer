Aegiphila plicata

Aegiphila plicata, commonly known as the zigzag mallow, is a flowering plant native to South America.

It is characterized by its distinctive zigzagging stems and heart-shaped leaves. The plant produces small, pinkish-white flowers that bloom in clusters during the summer months. It thrives in dry, rocky soils and is often found in arid or semi-arid regions.

Aegiphila plicata is valued for its ornamental appeal and is sometimes cultivated in gardens for its unique appearance.

Morphology

Aegiphila plicata has leaves that are narrow, lance-shaped, and arranged alternately along the stem, with a silvery-gray color due to a dense covering of fine hairs.

The stem is slender, with a smooth, light green bark that grows upright and branching. Its flowers are small, tubular, and clustered in dense inflorescences, typically pink or purple in color, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small capsule that splits open to release tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.
