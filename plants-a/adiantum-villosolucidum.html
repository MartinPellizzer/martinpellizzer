Adiantum villosolucidum

Adiantum villosolucidum, commonly known as the silky water fern, is a delicate fern species native to the mountainous regions of South America.

It is characterized by its finely divided, silky-hairy fronds that give it a soft, almost ethereal appearance. The plant typically grows in moist, shaded environments, often near streams or in rocky crevices. Its fronds are bi-lobed and have a distinctive translucent quality, especially when viewed in sunlight.

Adiantum villosolucidum is valued in horticulture for its ornamental appeal and ability to thrive in humid, indoor conditions.

Morphology

Adiantum villosolucidum has fern-like leaves that are pinnately compound, with a delicate, lace-like appearance, arranged alternately along the stem, and typically ranging from 10 to 30 cm in length, displaying a pale green to silvery-white color due to their fine, silky hairs.

The stem is slender, with a smooth, light green bark that grows upright and is often creeping along the ground. It produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are clustered in spore-bearing structures called sori, which are located on the underside of the leaves, and it does not produce true flowers in the traditional sense. The plant does not form fruits or seeds, as it reproduces via spores released from the sori.

Its roots are fibrous and adventitious, anchoring the plant to moist soil and aiding in water absorption.
