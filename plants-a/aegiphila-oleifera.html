Aegiphila oleifera

Aegiphila oleifera, commonly known as the Brazilian tea tree, is a shrub native to the Atlantic Forest region of Brazil.

It is characterized by its aromatic, hairy leaves and small, yellowish-green flowers. The plant is known for its essential oils, which are extracted from its leaves and used in aromatherapy and traditional medicine. It thrives in well-drained soils and is adapted to humid, tropical climates.

Aegiphila oleifera plays an important role in local ecosystems and is also valued for its ornamental qualities.

Morphology

Aegiphila oleifera has leaves that are opposite in arrangement, ovate to elliptical in shape, and range in size from 2 to 5 centimeters in length.

The leaves are usually green with a slightly pubescent surface and a dull texture. The stem is cylindrical, with a rough, reddish-brown bark that becomes more fissured with age. The plant produces small, clustered white or pale yellow flowers with five petals, blooming during the dry season, and is pollinated primarily by insects.

The fruit is a small, dry capsule that disperses seeds through wind and animal activity, and the roots are fibrous, helping the plant anchor in rocky or sandy soils.
