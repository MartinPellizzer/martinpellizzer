Aconitum arctoalpinum

Aconitum arctoalpinum, commonly known as the alpine aconite, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Europe and Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking blue to purple spires of showy flowers. The plant has large, deeply lobed leaves that are often covered in a dense layer of fine hairs. It thrives in cool, moist environments and is often found in alpine meadows and rocky slopes.

Aconitum arctoalpinum is noted for its toxic properties, containing potent alkaloids that can be harmful to humans and animals.

Morphology

Aconitum arctoalpinum has compound, doubly pinnate leaves that are dark green on top and silver-white underneath, arranged alternately along the stem, with each leaf reaching up to 30 cm in length.

The stem is smooth and somewhat hairy, with a greenish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age, and it grows upright with a somewhat woody base. The flowers are blue to purple, bell-shaped, and arranged in dense spikes, blooming from late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated primarily by insects such as bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, flat seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, helping the plant store nutrients and survive in its alpine habitat.
