Alberta loranthoides f. linearifolia

Alberta loranthoides f. linearifolia, commonly known as the Alberta mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of its host, typically lodgepole pine.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while producing its own sugars through photosynthesis using light that penetrates its host's canopy. The plant has a distinctive appearance with small, scale-like leaves and a dense, spherical cluster of berries that turn red when mature. It is native to the mountainous regions of Alberta, Canada, and is often found in forested areas with suitable host trees.

Its botanical structure reflects its parasitic lifestyle, with specialized structures for attachment and nutrient absorption.
