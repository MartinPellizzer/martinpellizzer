Aconitum engadinense

Aconitum engadinense, commonly known as the Engadine monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the alpine regions of Europe.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking violet-blue flowers arranged in a helmet-like shape. The plant features palmately compound leaves with deep lobes, and it thrives in moist, well-drained soils in mountainous areas. It is notable for containing potent alkaloids, making it toxic to both humans and animals.

This species is often found in meadows and rocky slopes at high altitudes.

Morphology

Aconitum engadinense has large, compound leaves that are broadly ovate to elliptical in shape, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter, hairy underside.

The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem, which is erect and somewhat woody, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark. The flowers are blue to purple, forming dense clusters at the top of the plant, and are pollinated by insects such as bees and butterflies; they bloom from late summer to early autumn. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, flat seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and fibrous, anchoring the plant firmly in the soil.
