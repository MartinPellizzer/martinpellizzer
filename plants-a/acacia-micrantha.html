Acacia micrantha

Acacia micrantha, commonly known as the narrow-leaved wattle, is a small to medium-sized shrub or tree native to southern Africa.

It is characterized by its slender, often multi-stemmed form and its feathery, bipinnate leaves that are typically narrow and linear. The plant produces small, yellow floral spikes that are typical of the Acacia genus, and these flowers are often followed by flat, pod-like fruits. Acacia micrantha is well adapted to arid and semi-arid environments, making it a resilient component of many local ecosystems.

It is also valued for its ornamental appeal and is sometimes used in traditional medicine.

Native Distribution

Acacia micrantha is primarily native to eastern and southern Africa, including countries such as Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, and South Africa.

It is commonly found in savannas, grasslands, and woodland areas where the climate is warm and semi-arid. This species thrives in well-drained soils and is often associated with rocky or sandy habitats. It plays an important ecological role by providing food and shelter for various wildlife species.

Acacia micrantha is also known for its ability to fix nitrogen, contributing to soil fertility in its native range.

Introduced Distribution

Acacia micrantha was mainly introduced to South Africa, where it has become an invasive species in certain regions.
