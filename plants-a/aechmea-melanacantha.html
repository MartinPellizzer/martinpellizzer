Aechmea melanacantha

Aechmea melanacantha, commonly known as the black-tongued bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its dense, rosette-shaped foliage that forms a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant features dark green, leathery leaves with a distinctive blackish or purplish band along the midrib, giving it its common name. It produces striking, showy inflorescences with vibrant red bracts and yellow flowers.

Aechmea melanacantha thrives in humid, shaded environments and is often cultivated for its ornamental value.

Morphology

Aechmea melanacantha has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, leathery, and dark green with a striking red or purple margin.

The leaves are arranged in a dense spiral, forming a compact rosette that holds water, which is typical of bromeliads. The plant has a short, fibrous stem that is often hidden beneath the leaves, with a rough, bark-like texture. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix, surrounded by showy red bracts, and bloom in late summer to early fall, pollinated primarily by insects.

The fruit is a three-lobed, fleshy berry that disperses its seeds through animal consumption and wind.
