Adiantum rodriguezii

Adiantum rodriguezii, commonly known as the Rodriguez's maidenhair fern, is a delicate fern species native to the mountainous regions of Southeast Asia.

It features intricate, lance-shaped fronds with a distinctive bipinnate structure, giving it a finely divided, lacy appearance. The plant produces small, spore-bearing structures called sori on the underside of its fronds, typical of the fern family. It thrives in shaded, moist environments and is often found in forest understories.

Due to its ornamental value, it is occasionally cultivated as a houseplant or in botanical gardens.

Morphology

Adiantum rodriguezii has fern-like leaves that are pinnately divided, with a delicate, feather-like appearance, and are typically arranged alternately along the stem.

The leaves are usually dark green, with a glossy surface, and can grow up to 30 centimeters in length. The plant has a slender, upright stem that is usually green and smooth, with a growth pattern that is mostly rhizomatous. It produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are typically greenish or brownish, blooming in the spring, and are pollinated by wind.
