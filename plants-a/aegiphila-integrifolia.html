Aegiphila integrifolia

Aegiphila integrifolia, commonly known as the broad-leaved aegiphila, is a shrub native to the Caribbean, particularly found in Puerto Rico and the Lesser Antilles.

It is characterized by its aromatic, lance-shaped leaves that are typically smooth and have a glossy appearance. The plant produces small, clustered white or pale yellow flowers that bloom in the summer months. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in coastal areas or near streams.

Due to its unique appearance and habitat, it is of interest to botanists and horticulturists alike.

Morphology

Aegiphila integrifolia has simple, lance-shaped leaves that are arranged alternately along the stem, with a smooth, glossy green upper surface and a lighter, hairy underside.

The stem is erect and woody, with a rough, grayish-brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are small, clustered in spikes, with pinkish-white petals and yellow stamens, blooming in spring and attracting insects for pollination. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in sandy or rocky soils.
