Aegiphila cordata

Aegiphila cordata, commonly known as the heartleaf aegiphila, is a perennial shrub native to the Caribbean region.

It is characterized by its heart-shaped leaves, which are often hairy and have a distinct, aromatic scent. The plant produces small, yellowish flowers that bloom in clusters during the spring and summer months. It thrives in well-drained soils and is often found in coastal areas and dry forests.

Due to its unique appearance and ecological significance, it is sometimes cultivated in botanical gardens for its ornamental value.

Morphology

Aegiphila cordata has heart-shaped, glossy leaves that are arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is slender and upright, with a smooth, pale brown bark that remains relatively unchanged as the plant matures. Its small, clustered flowers are pink to purple in color, with five petals and a tubular structure, blooming in spring and being pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a small capsule that splits open to release seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, helping the plant anchor itself in dry, rocky soils.
