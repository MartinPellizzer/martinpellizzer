Aconitum decorum

Aconitum decorum, commonly known as the velvetleaf monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to Europe.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, hood-shaped blue to purple flowers. The plant features large, palmately lobed leaves that are deeply divided and covered in a soft, velvety texture. Aconitum decorum typically grows in moist, shaded woodland areas and can reach heights of up to 1.5 meters.

It is known for its toxic properties, containing potent alkaloids that make it dangerous to both humans and animals.

Morphology

Aconitum decorum has large, palmately compound leaves that are deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes hairy underside.

The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem, which is upright, smooth, and has a grayish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age. The plant produces showy blue to purple flowers in late summer, with petals arranged in a spurred structure, and they are pollinated primarily by bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are deep and fibrous, adapted for anchoring the plant and absorbing nutrients from the soil.
