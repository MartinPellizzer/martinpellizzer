Aechmea stephanophora

Aechmea stephanophora, commonly known as the velvet bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients from the environment. The leaves are thick, waxy, and often have a velvety texture, giving the plant its common name. It produces vibrant red or orange inflorescences that emerge from the center of the rosette.

This plant thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular as a decorative houseplant due to its unique appearance and low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea stephanophora has rosette-shaped leaves that are narrow, sword-like, and arranged in a dense spiral, with a dark green color and a waxy surface.

The plant has a short, fibrous stem with a thick, smooth bark that is typically greenish-brown in color. Its inflorescence consists of a tall, branching spike of small, white to pale pink flowers that bloom during the wet season, and are pollinated by wind and insects. The fruit is a three-lobed capsule that disperses seeds by wind and water.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in the soil, with some rhizomes aiding in vegetative propagation.
