Aechmea friedrichsthalii

Aechmea friedrichsthalii, commonly known as the Friedrichsthal's bromeliad, is a striking plant native to the rainforests of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect water. The plant features vibrant, long-lasting bracts that come in shades of red, orange, and yellow, often mistaken for flowers. It thrives in humid, shaded environments and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Aechmea friedrichsthalii is popular as a decorative plant due to its ornamental foliage and unique water-storing structure.

Morphology

Aechmea friedrichsthalii has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, fleshy, and dark green with a waxy coating, arranged in a compact spiral at the base.

The stem is short and often hidden beneath the leaves, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that remains relatively inconspicuous. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, producing white to yellowish flowers that bloom during the dry season and are primarily pollinated by insects. The plant produces a fleshy, berry-like fruit that contains numerous small, black seeds, which are dispersed by birds and other animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the surrounding substrate, typical of bromeliads.
