Achillea schroeteri

Achillea schroeteri, commonly known as Schröter's yarrow, is a flowering plant native to the Mediterranean region.

It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is characterized by its flat, composite flower heads composed of numerous small florets. The plant typically grows in rocky or dry soils and is often found in coastal areas. It has finely divided, silver-green leaves that are aromatic and covered in fine hairs.

Schröter's yarrow is known for its medicinal properties and has been used traditionally for its antiseptic and anti-inflammatory qualities.

Morphology

Achillea schroeteri has basal leaves that are lance-shaped, gray-green in color, and have a fuzzy texture, arranged in a rosette formation and typically measuring up to 10 cm in length.

The stem is upright, hairy, and reddish-brown in color, growing from the base and branching sparingly. The flowers are compound umbels with yellow-green florets, blooming from late spring to early summer, and are pollinated primarily by insects such as bees. The fruit is a dry achene, with small, wind-dispersed seeds that are often carried by the wind.
