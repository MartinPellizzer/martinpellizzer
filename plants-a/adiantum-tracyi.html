Adiantum tracyi

Adiantum tracyi, commonly known as the Tracy's maidenhair fern, is a species of fern native to the southeastern United States.

It belongs to the family Adiantaceae and is characterized by its delicate, finely divided fronds that resemble lace. The plant typically grows in moist, shaded environments such as forests and stream banks. Its leaves are pinnately divided and have a distinctive blackish midrib.

Adiantum tracyi is valued for its ornamental appeal and is often cultivated in gardens for its aesthetic qualities.

Morphology

Adiantum tracyi has fern-like leaves that are pinnately divided, with a delicate, lace-like appearance, arranged alternately along the stem, and typically ranging from 10 to 20 centimeters in length.

The leaves are usually dark green on top and lighter green underneath, with a smooth texture. The stem is slender, upright, and somewhat woody, with a smooth, pale brown bark that grows in a branching pattern. Adiantum tracyi produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are greenish-white in color, blooming in early spring, and are wind-pollinated.
