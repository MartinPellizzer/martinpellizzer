Aegochloa pubescens

Aegochloa pubescens, commonly known as hairy panicgrass, is a perennial grass species native to tropical and subtropical regions.

It is characterized by its tufted growth habit and hairy leaves, which give it its common name. The plant typically grows in wetland environments, including marshes and swampy areas. Its inflorescence consists of dense, spike-like clusters of small, two-lobed flowers.

Aegochloa pubescens is valued for its ecological role in stabilizing soil and providing habitat for various wildlife species.

Morphology

Aegochloa pubescens has narrow, linear leaves that are typically arranged in a grass-like manner, with a bluish-green color and a rough, hairy texture on the upper surface.

The stems are slender, upright, and often have a light green to reddish-brown color, with a cylindrical shape and a growth pattern that is clump-forming. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences with inconspicuous, greenish flowers that bloom during the summer months, and they are primarily pollinated by wind. The fruit is a small, three-angled spikelet that disperses its seeds through wind, allowing the plant to spread in open, disturbed areas.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, helping the plant to anchor itself and propagate vegetatively.
