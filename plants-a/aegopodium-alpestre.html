Aegopodium alpestre

Aegopodium alpestre, commonly known as alpine ground-elder, is a herbaceous perennial plant native to mountainous regions of Europe and Asia.

It features a creeping rhizome system that allows it to spread and colonize rocky or disturbed soils. The plant produces small, white to greenish-yellow flowers arranged in umbels, typically blooming in late spring to early summer. Its leaves are palmately lobed and covered with fine hairs, giving them a fuzzy texture.

Aegopodium alpestre is often found in alpine meadows and rocky habitats, where it thrives in cool, moist conditions.

Morphology

Aegopodium alpestre has compound leaves that are trifoliate, with three oval to lance-shaped leaflets arranged oppositely along the stem, typically ranging from 5 to 15 cm in length, and are usually green with a slight hairy texture.

The stem is erect, hairy, and has a reddish-brown color, growing in a branching pattern. The flowers are small, white or pale yellow, clustered in umbels, and bloom from June to August, primarily pollinated by insects. The fruit is a dry, three-lobed achene, with the seeds dispersed by wind due to their lightweight structure.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, helping the plant to spread and establish itself in various habitats.
