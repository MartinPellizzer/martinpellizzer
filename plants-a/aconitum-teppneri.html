Aconitum teppneri

Aconitum teppneri, commonly known as the Siberian monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It features striking blue-violet to purple flowers arranged in a helmet-like shape, characteristic of the genus Aconitum. The plant has palmately divided leaves that are deeply lobed, contributing to its ornamental appeal. It thrives in well-drained soil and full sun to partial shade, making it a popular choice for rock gardens.

However, it is highly toxic due to the presence of potent alkaloids, requiring caution during handling and cultivation.

Morphology

Aconitum teppneri has leaves that are compound, with palmate or pinnate divisions, arranged alternately along the stem, and typically have a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is usually erect, with a smooth, dark brown bark that is somewhat rough to the touch. The flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, with five petals and a tall spire-like structure, blooming in late summer to early autumn and pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, helping the plant to store nutrients and survive in its native environment.
