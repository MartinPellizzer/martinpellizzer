Aechmea fraudulosa

Aechmea fraudulosa, commonly known as the false pineapple, is a striking bromeliad native to the subtropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater to support its growth. The plant produces vibrant, spathaceous flowers that range in color from red to orange, often appearing in late spring or early summer. Its foliage is typically dark green with a waxy texture, and it thrives in bright, indirect light.

Aechmea fraudulosa is popular as a decorative houseplant due to its unique appearance and low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea fraudulosa has leaves that are typically broad, strap-shaped, and arranged in a rosette formation, with a waxy, dark green to blue-green coloration.

The stem is short and often hidden beneath the leaf rosette, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that remains relatively inconspicuous. Its flowers are borne in a dense, spikelike inflorescence, featuring vibrant red or orange bracts that enclose small, white to yellowish flowers, blooming during the dry season and pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a fleshy, red or orange berry that contains numerous small seeds, which are dispersed by animals consuming the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and adapted to absorb moisture from the surrounding soil, supporting the plant's growth in humid, shaded environments.
