Aegiphila farinosa

Aegiphila farinosa, commonly known as the velvetleaf or sand verbena, is a herbaceous plant native to the southwestern United States and northern Mexico.

It is characterized by its hairy, grayish-green foliage and small, white or pale pink flowers that bloom in late spring to early summer. The plant thrives in well-drained soils and full sun, often found in rocky or arid environments. Its leaves are covered in fine, white pubescence, which helps reduce water loss in dry conditions.

Aegiphila farinosa is often used in native plant gardens for its drought tolerance and ornamental value.

Morphology

Aegiphila farinosa has leaves that are narrow, lance-shaped, and covered in a fine, powdery coating, giving them a grayish-white color.

The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem, which is slender, woody, and has a rough, fissured bark. The flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, with pinkish-white petals and yellow stamens, blooming in spring and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.
