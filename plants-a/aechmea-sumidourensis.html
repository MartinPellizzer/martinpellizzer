Aechmea sumidourensis

Aechmea sumidourensis, commonly known as the silver pine bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical rainforests of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater and nutrients. The leaves are typically broad, waxy, and have a silver-blue hue, giving the plant its common name. It produces striking flower spikes that emerge from the center of the rosette, often in shades of red or orange.

This plant thrives in bright, indirect light and requires high humidity to flourish.

Morphology

Aechmea sumidourensis has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, stiff, and pointed, with a dark green color and a waxy surface, arranged in a tight spiral around the plant's center.

The stem is short and thick, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that grows from the base of the rosette. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix, surrounded by colorful bracts, with purple and green hues, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and form a dense network around the base of the plant, anchoring it and absorbing moisture from the environment.
