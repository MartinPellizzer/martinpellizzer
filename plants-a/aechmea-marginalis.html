Aechmea marginalis

Aechmea marginalis, commonly known as the marginal aechmea, is a bromeliad native to Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences that range in color from red to orange. It thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil.

Aechmea marginalis is often used in ornamental gardening due to its attractive foliage and flowering display.

Morphology

Aechmea marginalis has rosette-shaped leaves that are narrow, sword-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a waxy blue-green color and a sharp, pointed tip.

The plant has a short, thick stem covered in a fibrous, grayish-brown bark that grows upright from the base. Its flowers are clustered in a spike-like inflorescence, consisting of bright red bracts with yellowish-orange tubular flowers, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds. The fruit is a small, fleshy capsule that turns red when mature, dispersing seeds through wind and animal consumption.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in the soil and absorbing moisture from the air.
