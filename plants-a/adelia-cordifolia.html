Adelia cordifolia

Adelia cordifolia, commonly known as the heart-leaved adelia, is a flowering plant native to the arid regions of southern Africa.

It is characterized by its heart-shaped leaves, which are typically thick and fleshy to store water. The plant produces clusters of small, fragrant white or pale yellow flowers during the cooler months. It thrives in well-drained soils and requires minimal water, making it well adapted to its harsh environment.

Due to its unique appearance and resilience, it is often cultivated in gardens with suitable conditions.

Morphology

Adelia cordifolia has leaves that are broadly heart-shaped (cordate), arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside, measuring approximately 5-10 cm in length.

The stem is slender, smooth, and slightly woody, with a light brown bark that becomes fissured with age, growing upright with few branches. Its flowers are small, clustered in terminal inflorescences, featuring five pink petals and a yellow center, blooming in spring, and are pollinated primarily by bees. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in moist, well-drained soils.
