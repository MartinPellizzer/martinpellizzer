Achillea similis

Achillea similis, commonly known as field yarrow, is a flowering plant belonging to the Asteraceae family.

It is characterized by its flat, composite flower heads that typically range from white to pale pink in color. The plant has feathery, aromatic leaves that are finely divided and covered in fine hairs. It is native to Europe and parts of western Asia, often found in meadows and grasslands.

Achillea similis is known for its medicinal properties and has been used historically for its anti-inflammatory and astringent effects.

Morphology

Achillea similis has basal leaves that are lance-shaped, grey-green in color, and have a rough texture, arranged in a rosette formation.

The stem is upright, slender, and slightly hairy, with a light green color and a branching growth pattern. Its flowers are composite, consisting of yellow disk flowers surrounded by white ray flowers, blooming in late spring to early summer, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small, dry achene with a pappus for wind dispersal.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, allowing the plant to spread vegetatively.
