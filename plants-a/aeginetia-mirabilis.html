Aeginetia mirabilis

Aeginetia mirabilis, commonly known as the silky hibiscus, is a flowering plant native to tropical regions of Africa.

It is characterized by its striking, velvety purple flowers that bloom in clusters. The plant has heart-shaped leaves with a soft, hairy texture. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers full sun to partial shade.

Aeginetia mirabilis is often cultivated for its ornamental value and is also used in traditional medicine.

Morphology

Aeginetia mirabilis has leaves that are ovate to elliptical in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, with a dull green color and a moderate size, typically ranging from 2 to 5 centimeters in length.

The stem is slender and somewhat woody, with a smooth, light green bark that becomes rougher with age, growing in a creeping or ascending pattern. Its flowers are small, greenish-white, and clustered in umbels, blooming from late spring to early summer, and are pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a dry, three-lobed capsule that splits open to release tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, helping the plant anchor itself in sandy or rocky soils.
