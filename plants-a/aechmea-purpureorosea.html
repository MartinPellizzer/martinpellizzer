Aechmea purpureorosea

Aechmea purpureorosea, commonly known as the pink rose bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences that range in color from pink to red, often with a rose-like appearance. Its foliage is typically broad and sword-shaped, with a waxy texture that aids in water retention.

Aechmea purpureorosea is well-suited for indoor cultivation due to its adaptability and ornamental value.

Morphology

Aechmea purpureorosea has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, fleshy, and covered in fine, white hairs, with a dark green to purple coloration on the upper surface and a lighter, sometimes reddish underside.

The leaves are arranged in a compact rosette, often reaching lengths of up to 30 cm, and are stiff and upright. The plant lacks a true stem, with the base of the leaves forming a tight cluster that resembles a pseudostem. Its inflorescence is a spadix enclosed in a colorful spathe, with small, white to pale pink flowers that bloom in late summer to early autumn, primarily pollinated by insects.

The fruit is a fleshy, red berry that contains numerous small seeds, which are dispersed by birds and other animals.
