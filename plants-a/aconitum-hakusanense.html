Aconitum hakusanense

Aconitum hakusanense, commonly known as the Japanese monkshood, is a herbaceous perennial plant native to Japan.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, hood-shaped blue or purple flowers. The plant typically grows to a height of 1 to 2 meters and features palmately divided leaves. Its flowers are arranged in racemes, and the plant thrives in moist, well-drained soils.

Aconitum hakusanense is notable for its toxicity, containing potent alkaloids that can be harmful to humans and animals.

Morphology

Aconitum hakusanense has leaves that are palmately compound, with 5-7 leaflets arranged in a fan-like pattern, each leaflet being ovate to lanceolate in shape, and varying in size from 5 to 15 cm in length.

The leaves are typically dark green on the upper surface and lighter green on the underside, with a hairy texture. The stem is upright and branched, with a rough, dark brown bark that has a slightly fibrous texture. The flowers are blue to purple in color, arranged in dense spikes, and consist of five petals with a spurred lower petal; they bloom in late summer to early autumn and are pollinated by insects such as bees.

The fruit is a follicle that develops from a single carpel, containing small, flat, black seeds that are dispersed by wind.
