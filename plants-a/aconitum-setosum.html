Aconitum setosum

Aconitum setosum, commonly known as the hairy monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant belonging to the Ranunculaceae family.

It is characterized by its striking purple or violet flowers that are hooded and spurred, resembling a monk's habit, hence its common name. The plant features palmate leaves with lobes that are often covered in fine hairs, contributing to its distinctive appearance. Native to the mountainous regions of central and southern Europe, it thrives in moist, shaded environments.

Aconitum setosum contains toxic alkaloids, making it a plant that requires caution when handling.

Morphology

Aconitum setosum has compound, palmately lobed leaves that are typically green with a dark green underside, arranged alternately along the stem, and can grow up to 30 cm in length.

The stem is erect, hairy, and has a rough, dark brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are blue to purple, forming tall, sparsely branched clusters, and bloom in late summer to early autumn, pollinated primarily by insects. The plant produces follicles as its fruit, containing small, flat seeds that are dispersed by wind.

Its roots are tuberous and fibrous, adapted for storing nutrients and surviving harsh conditions.
