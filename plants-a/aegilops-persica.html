Aegilops persica

Aegilops persica, commonly known as Persian wheatgrass, is a perennial grass native to the Middle East and Central Asia.

It is characterized by its slender, grass-like foliage and produces small, spike-like inflorescences. The plant is closely related to wheat and is sometimes used in traditional medicine for its purported health benefits. It thrives in temperate climates and prefers well-drained soil.

Aegilops persica is also valued for its potential role in agricultural research due to its genetic relationship with cultivated wheat species.

Morphology

Aegilops persica has narrow, elongated leaves that are alternate in arrangement, typically ranging from 5 to 10 centimeters in length, with a bluish-green color and a slightly hairy texture.

Its stem is slender and erect, with a smooth, light green bark that grows straight upward. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences bearing pale green to white flowers, which bloom in early spring and are pollinated by wind. The fruit is a three-lobed spike that disperses its seeds by wind once matured.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, helping the plant anchor in sandy or dry soils.
