Aechmea murcae

Aechmea murcae, commonly known as the Murcia bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to Central and South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant's foliage is typically green with a reddish or purple tinge, especially under bright light conditions. Aechmea murcae produces vibrant orange or red flowers that emerge from the leaf rosette, adding visual interest to its appearance.

It thrives in humid environments and is often cultivated as an ornamental plant due to its aesthetic appeal.

Morphology

Aechmea murcae has rosette-shaped leaves that are narrow, strap-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a waxy blue-green color and a sharp, pointed tip.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, thick, and fibrous base that resembles a pseudostem. Its inflorescence is a spike-like structure with small, violet flowers that bloom during the wet season, primarily pollinated by insects. The fruit is a three-lobed capsule that disperses its seeds through wind and animal ingestion.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring in the soil, with some aerial roots that absorb moisture from the air.
