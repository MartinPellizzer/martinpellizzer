Aechmea anomala

Aechmea anomala, commonly known as the silver vase plant, is a striking bromeliad native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater to support its flowering structure. The plant produces vibrant orange or red flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette, often lasting several months. Its foliage is typically covered in a silvery or white powdery coating, giving it a unique appearance.

Aechmea anomala is well-suited for indoor cultivation due to its tolerance for low light and ability to thrive in humid environments.

Morphology

Aechmea anomala has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, strap-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a waxy blue-green color and a pointed tip.

The plant lacks a typical stem, with a short, thick base that is often covered in a fibrous, spongy material resembling bark. Its inflorescence is a tall, spadix surrounded by a spathe, with small, white to pale pink flowers that bloom from late winter to early spring, pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a capsule that produces small, black seeds dispersed by wind and animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted to anchor the plant in its epiphytic or terrestrial habitat.
