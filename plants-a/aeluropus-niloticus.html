Aeluropus niloticus

Aeluropus niloticus, commonly known as the Nile grass, is a perennial grass species native to the wetlands of Africa, the Middle East, and parts of Asia.

It is characterized by its tufted growth habit and narrow, grass-like leaves that are often covered in fine hairs. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences that bear numerous tiny flowers, which are typically wind-pollinated. Aeluropus niloticus thrives in saline and alkaline soils, making it an important component of coastal and marsh ecosystems.

It is also valued for its role in stabilizing soil and preventing erosion in wetland areas.

Morphology

Aeluropus niloticus has grass-like leaves that are narrow, linear, and slightly rolled, arranged in a basal rosette with a bluish-green color and a rough texture.

The stem is erect, slender, and slightly hairy, with a greenish hue, and grows in dense tufts or clumps. The flowers are small, spike-like clusters with pale green to yellowish bracts, blooming during the dry season, and are wind-pollinated. The fruit is a three-sided, dry capsule that disperses seeds by wind after splitting open.

The roots are fibrous and extensive, helping to stabilize soil and absorb water efficiently.
