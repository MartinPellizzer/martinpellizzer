Acer acinatum

Acer acinatum, commonly known as the maple-leaved elm, is a deciduous tree native to the eastern United States.

It belongs to the Sapindaceae family and is characterized by its distinctive, palmately lobed leaves that resemble those of maple trees. The tree produces small, yellowish flowers in early spring, followed by spherical, greenish fruits that turn red as they mature. Its bark is smooth and grayish-brown, becoming fissured with age, and it typically grows to a height of 20 to 30 meters.

The plant is valued for its ornamental appeal and historical use in traditional medicine.
