Aechmea paradoxa

Aechmea paradoxa, commonly known as the velvet leaf bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette of fleshy, dark green leaves that form a central cup to collect water. The leaves are covered in a dense, velvety coating that gives the plant its common name. It produces vibrant orange or red flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette.

This plant thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea paradoxa has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, stiff, and often have a waxy texture, arranged in a compact circle around the central rosette.

The leaves are typically blue-green in color, with a glossy surface and can grow up to 60 cm in length. The plant has a short, thick, underground stem with a fibrous, reddish-brown bark that grows from the base of the rosette.

Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a spathe, with small, clustered flowers that are usually pink or red, blooming in the summer months and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a berry that contains numerous small seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the air and soil.
