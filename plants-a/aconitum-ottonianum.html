Aconitum ottonianum

Aconitum ottonianum, commonly known as the Emperor's Crown, is a striking perennial plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stems that can reach up to 2 meters in height. The plant produces showy, violet-blue to purple flowers in late summer, arranged in a dense, spire-like cluster at the top of the stem. Its leaves are deeply lobed and have a waxy appearance, typical of many members of the buttercup family.

Aconitum ottonianum is often cultivated in gardens for its ornamental value and dramatic visual impact.

Morphology

Aconitum ottonianum has leaves that are compound, consisting of several lance-shaped leaflets arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is tall and erect, with a smooth, bluish-green bark that becomes rougher with age. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, with spurred petals and a central nectar-secreting spur, blooming in late summer and attracting pollinators like bees and butterflies through entomophily. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds dispersed by wind.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, adapted for absorbing nutrients from the soil.
