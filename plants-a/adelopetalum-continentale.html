Adelopetalum continentale

Adelopetalum continentale, commonly known as the desert bellflower, is a perennial flowering plant native to arid regions of Central Asia.

It is characterized by its low-growing, mat-forming habit and hairy stems that help reduce water loss in hot, dry environments. The plant produces showy, bell-shaped flowers in shades of pink or white, which bloom in late spring to early summer. Its leaves are small, narrow, and covered in fine hairs, adapted to minimize transpiration.

Adelopetalum continentale is often used in xeriscaping due to its drought tolerance and ornamental value.

Morphology

Adelopetalum continentale has simple, alternate leaves that are elliptical in shape, with a smooth, dark green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes hairy underside.

The stem is slender and cylindrical, with a smooth, pale green bark that becomes rougher with age, and it grows upright with a branching pattern. The flowers are small, clustered in panicles, with white petals and yellow stamens, blooming in late spring to early summer, and they are pollinated by insects.
