Aconitum parahakonense

Aconitum parahakonense, commonly known as the Parahakon blue monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of southern China.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking purple-blue flowers arranged in a helmet-like shape. The plant features large, palmately lobed leaves that are deeply cut and have a velvety texture. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it should be handled with care and is often cultivated for its ornamental value in gardens.

Morphology

Aconitum parahakonense has large, compound leaves that are palmately arranged, with deeply lobed blades that are dark green on top and lighter underneath, typically reaching up to 20 cm in length.

The stem is erect and cylindrical, with a smooth, dark brown bark that becomes rougher with age, and it grows in a straight, upright manner. The flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, with five petal-like sepals and numerous yellow stamens, blooming in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, adapted for storage and anchoring the plant in its habitat.
