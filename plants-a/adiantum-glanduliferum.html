Adiantum glanduliferum

Adiantum glanduliferum, commonly known as the glandular maidenhair fern, is a species of fern native to eastern North America.

It is characterized by its finely divided, delicate fronds that are typically dark green with a slightly glossy appearance. The plant produces small, spore-bearing structures called sori on the underside of its leaves. It thrives in moist, shaded environments and is often found in woodland areas.

Adiantum glanduliferum is valued for its ornamental appeal and is sometimes cultivated in gardens.

Adiantum glanduliferum has delicate, finely divided leaves that are typically triangular in shape, arranged in a pinnate pattern, and measure about 10-20 cm in length, with a silvery-green color due to the presence of trichomes.

The stem is slender and upright, with a smooth, light green bark that grows in a rosette formation. It produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are clustered in spore-bearing structures called sori, which are located on the underside of the leaves, and they do not produce showy flowers in the traditional sense. The plant does not produce true fruits or seeds; instead, it reproduces via spores released from the sori, which are dispersed by wind.

Its roots are fibrous and help anchor the plant while absorbing nutrients from the soil.

Native Distribution

Adiantum glanduliferum is primarily native to eastern North America, including the United States and parts of Canada.

It is commonly found in moist, wooded areas such as forests and stream sides. This fern thrives in shaded environments with rich, well-drained soil. It is particularly common in the Appalachian region and the southeastern United States.

Its native range extends from New England down to the Gulf Coast.
