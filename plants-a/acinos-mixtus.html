Acinos mixtus

Acinos mixtus, commonly known as the alpine bellflower, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Europe.

It is characterized by its compact, mat-forming growth habit and produces small, bell-shaped flowers in shades of white, pink, and purple. The plant thrives in rocky, well-drained soils and is adapted to cold, high-altitude environments. Its leaves are typically narrow and hairy, aiding in water retention and protection from harsh weather.

Acinos mixtus plays an important ecological role in alpine ecosystems, providing food for various pollinators and contributing to soil stabilization.

Morphology

Acinos mixtus has compound leaves that are alternate in arrangement, with ovate to lanceolate leaflets that are usually gray-green in color and have a rough, hairy texture.

The stem is wiry and somewhat succulent, with a grayish-brown bark that becomes more fissured with age, and it grows in a prostrate or ascending manner. The flowers are small, white or pale yellow, with five petals and numerous stamens, blooming in early summer and pollinated by wind. The fruit is a follicle that releases seeds through dehiscence, and the seeds are small, winged, and dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapting to rocky or dry soils.
