Aechmea marmorata

Aechmea marmorata, commonly known as the Marble Aechmea, is a bromeliad native to Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central tank to collect water. The leaves are typically gray-green with white or silver markings, giving it a striking appearance. It produces vibrant orange or red flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette.

This plant thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea marmorata has leaves that are broad, strap-shaped, and arranged in a rosette formation, with a waxy, dark green surface and a striking silver or white stripe running along the midrib.

The plant lacks a true stem, instead having a short, compact growth pattern with a thick, fleshy base that resembles a pseudostem. Its inflorescence consists of a dense cluster of bright red flowers with yellowish tepals, blooming during the summer months and pollinated primarily by birds, especially hummingbirds. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and adapted to absorb moisture from the air, typical of its bromeliad family.
