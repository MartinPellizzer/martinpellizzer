Aechmea pubescens

Aechmea pubescens, commonly known as the velvet plant, is a bromeliad native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that are covered in a dense, velvety pubescence, giving it its common name. The plant produces striking inflorescences with brightly colored flowers, often in shades of red, orange, or yellow. Its leaves are typically broad and strap-like, with a waxy surface that helps retain moisture.

Aechmea pubescens is often grown as a decorative plant due to its unique appearance and ability to thrive in indirect light conditions.

Morphology

Aechmea pubescens has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, fleshy, and covered in a dense layer of white, woolly hairs, giving them a distinctive silvery appearance.

The leaves are arranged in a tight spiral, forming a central rosette, and can grow up to 30 centimeters in length. The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, underground rhizome that supports the rosette. Its inflorescence consists of a spike of small, red or orange flowers that bloom in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects such as bees and moths.

The fruit is a capsule that releases seeds through wind dispersal, and the roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the air.
