Aechmea guainumbiorum

Aechmea guainumbiorum, commonly known as the Guainumbi bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the rainforests of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant's foliage is typically a vibrant green with red or purple markings, enhancing its ornamental appeal. It produces striking inflorescences with colorful bracts that can range from yellow to red, often lasting for several months.

Aechmea guainumbiorum thrives in humid, shaded environments and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea guainumbiorum has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, leathery, and often have a bluish or grayish hue, arranged in a compact circle around the central stem.

The stem is short and thick, with a rough, fibrous texture and a reddish-brown color, growing upright from the base. Its inflorescence consists of a spike of small, colorful flowers, typically in shades of red, yellow, or orange, which bloom during the dry season and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a three-lobed capsule that releases small, black seeds through mechanical means when it dries and splits open.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the surrounding substrate.
