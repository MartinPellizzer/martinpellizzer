Aconitum bicolor

Aconitum bicolor, commonly known as the Western Monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Europe and Asia.

It is characterized by its striking, hood-shaped flowers that are typically blue or purple in color. The plant belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and features a tall, unbranched stem with palmate leaves. Its roots contain toxic alkaloids, making it a dangerous plant to handle.

Aconitum bicolor thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in woodland or alpine habitats.

Morphology

Aconitum bicolor has large, deeply lobed leaves that are broadly ovate to heart-shaped, with a velvety texture and a dark green upper surface contrasting with a lighter underside.

The stem is upright, smooth, and typically blue-green in color, growing in a solitary, unbranched pattern. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, with five petal-like sepals and a spurred structure, blooming in late summer to early autumn and pollinated by insects. The plant produces follicles as its fruit, containing small, black seeds that are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, adapted for storing nutrients and surviving harsh conditions.
