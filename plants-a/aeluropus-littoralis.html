Aeluropus littoralis

Aeluropus littoralis, commonly known as saltgrass, is a perennial grass native to coastal regions of Europe, Asia, and Africa.

It thrives in saline and brackish environments, often found in salt marshes and estuaries. The plant has narrow, grass-like leaves and produces small, spike-like inflorescences that contain minute grains of wheat-like seeds. It plays a vital role in stabilizing coastal soils and providing habitat for various wildlife species.

Its ability to tolerate high salinity makes it an important component of coastal ecosystems.

Morphology

Aeluropus littoralis has narrow, grass-like leaves that are flat, linear, and typically arranged in a spiral pattern along the stem.

The leaves are usually 2–5 cm long and 2–4 mm wide, with a blue-gray or silver-green color due to a dense covering of hairs. The stem is erect, cylindrical, and smooth, with a grayish-green color and a growth pattern that is usually clump-forming. It produces small, inconspicuous flowers in dense spike-like clusters, typically blooming in late spring to early summer, and is pollinated by wind.

The fruit is a three-angled, three-lobed spikelet that disperses its seeds by wind, and the roots are fibrous and well-developed, helping the plant stabilize in sandy or coastal soils.
