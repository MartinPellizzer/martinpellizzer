Adyseton savranicum

Adyseton savranicum, commonly known as the red sandalwood, is a flowering plant native to parts of Southeast Asia.

It is characterized by its tall, straight trunk and dark reddish-brown bark. The tree produces small, fragrant flowers that are typically white or pale yellow. Its wood is highly valued for its durability and rich color, making it a sought-after material in traditional crafts.

However, due to overharvesting, it is now listed as an endangered species in many regions.

Morphology

Adyseton savranicum has alternate, lance-shaped leaves that are dark green on top and lighter underneath, with a smooth texture and a moderate size, typically up to 10 cm in length.

The stem is slender and slightly woody, with a grayish-brown bark that is smooth when young and becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are small, clustered in panicles, with pinkish-white petals and yellow stamens, blooming in early summer and pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapting to nutrient-poor soils.
