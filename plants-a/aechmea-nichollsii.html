Aechmea nichollsii

Aechmea nichollsii, commonly known as the Nicholls' pineapple bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of Central America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater and nutrients. The plant produces vibrant, spiky flower spikes that can range in color from red to orange, often resembling a pineapple. Its leaves are typically broad, leathery, and covered in a dense layer of trichomes, which help in water retention.

Aechmea nichollsii thrives in high humidity and indirect light, making it a popular choice for indoor cultivation.

Morphology

Aechmea nichollsii has rosette-shaped leaves that are narrow, sword-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a bright green color and a waxy coating.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, thick base that supports the rosette, and its leaves form a central cup that collects water. It produces showy, spadix-like inflorescences with white flowers that bloom in late winter to early spring, pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a red, fleshy berry containing numerous small seeds, which are dispersed by birds and water.

Its roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring in the soil, with some rhizomes extending to form new plants.
