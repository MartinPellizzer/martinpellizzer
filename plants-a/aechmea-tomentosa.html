Aechmea tomentosa

Aechmea tomentosa, commonly known as the woolly bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical rainforests of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that are covered in dense, woolly white trichomes, giving it a distinctive appearance. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences that range in color from red to orange, adding visual interest to its foliage. Its ability to thrive in humid, shaded environments makes it a popular choice for indoor gardening.

The plant's unique structure supports a microhabitat for various small organisms, contributing to its ecological significance.

Morphology

Aechmea tomentosa has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, thick, and covered in a dense white tomentum, giving them a fuzzy appearance.

The leaves are arranged in a tight spiral, forming a rosette that holds water, and they vary in size from 20 to 40 centimeters in length. The plant has a short, fibrous stem that is often hidden beneath the leaf rosette, with a light green to brownish bark. Its flowers are borne in a terminal spike, consisting of small, white or pale blue bracts that enclose the true flowers, which are yellowish and bloom during the summer months.

The flowers are pollinated primarily by insects, and the plant produces a capsule fruit containing numerous tiny seeds, which are dispersed by wind.
