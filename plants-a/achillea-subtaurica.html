Achillea subtaurica

Achillea subtaurica, commonly known as the Balkan yarrow, is a perennial herb native to the mountainous regions of southeastern Europe.

It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is characterized by its flat, composite flower heads that range in color from white to pale pink. The plant typically grows in rocky or dry habitats, showcasing its adaptability to harsh environments. Its foliage consists of finely divided, silvery-gray leaves that are aromatic and often used in traditional herbal medicine.

Achillea subtaurica is valued for its ornamental appeal and ecological role in supporting pollinators.

Morphology

Achillea subtaurica has leaves that are lanceolate to linear in shape, with a silvery-gray underside and a dark green upper surface, arranged in a basal rosette and typically measuring 5-10 cm in length.

The stems are erect, hairy, and have a greenish-gray bark, growing in a compact, low shrub-like form. The flowers are small, white to pale yellow, arranged in flat-topped clusters called cymes, blooming from late spring to early summer, and are pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a dry, cylindrical achene with a pappus, and the seeds are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, allowing the plant to spread vegetatively.
