Aconitum bartokianum

Aconitum bartokianum, commonly known as the Bartok's monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, hood-shaped blue to purple flowers. The plant features large, palmately lobed leaves that are deeply divided, giving it a distinctive appearance. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it is important to handle the plant with care.

Morphology

Aconitum bartokianum has large, compound leaves that are deeply lobed, arranged alternately along the stem, and typically have a dark green upper surface with a lighter underside.

The stem is tall and erect, with a smooth, bluish-green bark that becomes rougher with age. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, and consist of five petal-like sepals and two smaller petals, blooming in late summer to early autumn and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a follicle that releases seeds through a spiral opening, with seeds dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and form a dense network that helps anchor the plant and absorb nutrients from the soil.
