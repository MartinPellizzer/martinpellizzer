Aconitum bavaricum

Aconitum bavaricum, commonly known as the Bavarian aconite, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Europe.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stems that can reach up to 1.5 meters in height. The plant produces striking blue to purple flowers in late summer, arranged in dense clusters at the top of the stem. Its leaves are deeply lobed and have a distinctive, almost fern-like appearance.

Aconitum bavaricum is known for its toxic properties, containing various alkaloids that can be harmful to humans and animals.

Morphology

Aconitum bavaricum has compound, palmately arranged leaves that are deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside, typically ranging from 10 to 20 centimeters in diameter.

The stem is tall and erect, with a smooth, dark brown bark that becomes fissured with age, and it grows in a straight, unbranched pattern. The flowers are blue to purple in color, arranged in dense spikes, with five petal-like sepals and a showy, spurred calyx, and they bloom in late summer, pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, often forming a dense network underground.
