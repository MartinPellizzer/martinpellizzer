Aegiphila latifolia

Aegiphila latifolia, commonly known as the broadleaf aegiphila, is a flowering plant native to the Caribbean and parts of South America.

It belongs to the Lamiaceae family, which includes many aromatic herbs and shrubs. The plant is characterized by its lance-shaped leaves and clusters of small, tubular flowers that range in color from white to pink. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in forest understories or along stream banks.

Due to its ornamental value and medicinal properties, it is sometimes cultivated in gardens and traditional herbal practices.

Morphology

Aegiphila latifolia has simple, alternate, ovate to elliptic leaves that are typically dark green on top and lighter green underneath, with a smooth texture and a moderate size.

The stem is erect and somewhat woody, with a grayish-brown bark that is smooth when young and develops fissures as the plant matures. The flowers are small, clustered in umbels, and have a pale yellow color, blooming in late spring to early summer, and are primarily pollinated by insects. The fruit is a dry, capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapting to the plant's preference for well-drained soils in dry environments.
