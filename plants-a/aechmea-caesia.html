Aechmea caesia

Aechmea caesia, commonly known as the silver pine bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the humid forests of Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaf structure, which forms a central cup to collect rainwater. The leaves are typically long, narrow, and covered in a silvery-white powdery coating, giving it its common name. It produces vibrant orange to red inflorescences that bloom in the spring.

This plant is popular in horticulture for its ornamental value and ability to thrive in indirect light with minimal care.

Morphology

Aechmea caesia has leaves that are sword-shaped, arranged in a rosette, and can grow up to 60 cm in length, with a dark green to bluish-gray coloration.

The plant has a short, unbranched stem that is covered in a fibrous, reddish-brown bark. Its inflorescence consists of a dense spike of bright red flowers, which bloom during the summer months and are pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds. The fruit is a capsule that contains small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind and water.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in its epiphytic or terrestrial habitat.
