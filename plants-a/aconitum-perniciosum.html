Aconitum perniciosum

Aconitum perniciosum, commonly known as the deadly nightshade, is a member of the Ranunculaceae family.

It is a perennial herb that typically grows in moist, shady environments. The plant produces striking blue or purple flowers, which are followed by egg-shaped berries. Its roots and other parts contain toxic alkaloids, making it highly dangerous to humans and animals.

Despite its toxicity, it is sometimes cultivated for its ornamental value.

The stem is upright, rough to the touch, and has a grayish-brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are blue to purple, bell-shaped, and arranged in loose racemes, blooming in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, often forming a dense network underground.

Native Distribution

Aconitum perniciosum is primarily native to the mountainous regions of central and southern Europe, including countries such as Austria, Germany, Italy, and Switzerland.

It thrives in alpine and subalpine environments, often growing in rocky soils and open meadows at elevations ranging from 1,500 to 3,000 meters. This plant is commonly found in the Alps and the Carpathian Mountains, where it adapts well to cold climates and high altitudes.
