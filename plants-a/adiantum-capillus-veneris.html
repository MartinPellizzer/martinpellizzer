Adiantum capillus-veneris

Adiantum capillus-veneris, commonly known as the "Maiden's Hair Fern," is a delicate fern native to tropical and subtropical regions.

It features finely divided, silky fronds that give it a feathery appearance, with each leaflet being narrow and tapering. The plant thrives in moist, shady environments and has a preference for well-drained soil. Its reproductive structures, including sori, are located on the underside of the fronds, typical of ferns.

It is often cultivated for its ornamental value and is used in traditional medicine in various cultures.

Morphology

Adiantum capillus-veneris has delicate, finely divided leaves that are bi- or tri-pinnate, giving them a feathery appearance, with a bright green color and a wavy margin.

The leaves are arranged alternately along the stem, which is slender, creeping, and often hairy, with a light green to yellowish bark. It produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are greenish-white in color, blooming in late spring to early summer, and are wind-pollinated. The fruit is a dark brown, globose achene, dispersed by wind, and the seeds are tiny with a tuft of hair for aid in wind dispersal.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, helping the plant to spread and anchor itself in moist, shady environments.

Introduced Distribution

Adiantum capillus-veneris was mainly introduced to Austria, Belgium, Czechia-Slovakia, Germany, and the Netherlands.

It is commonly found in moist, shaded environments such as woodlands and along stream banks. The plant is often cultivated as an ornamental due to its delicate, fern-like appearance. Its introduction to these regions is largely attributed to horticultural trade and garden cultivation.

However, it is considered a potential invasive species in some areas where it can outcompete native vegetation.
