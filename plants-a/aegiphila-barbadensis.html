Aegiphila barbadensis

Aegiphila barbadensis, commonly known as the Caribbean senna, is a shrub native to the Caribbean islands.

It belongs to the Fabaceae family, which includes legumes, and is characterized by its compound leaves and yellow flowers. The plant is often found in coastal areas and dry forests, adapting to sandy and well-drained soils. It plays a role in local ecosystems by providing habitat and food for various insects and birds.

Its ornamental value also makes it a popular plant in horticulture.

Morphology

Aegiphila barbadensis has leaves that are lanceolate in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes hairy underside.

The stem is woody, with a rough, fissured bark that is typically grayish-brown in color, and it grows in a shrub-like form. Its flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, with tubular corollas that are typically pink or purple, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by birds. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and help anchor the plant in rocky or sandy soils.
