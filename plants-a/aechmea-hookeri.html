Aechmea hookeri

Aechmea hookeri, commonly known as the Hooker's Bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of Central and South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant's foliage is typically bright green with red or purple markings, and it produces striking inflorescences with vibrant orange or red flowers. It thrives in humid, shaded environments and requires well-draining soil or a substrate to prevent root rot.

Aechmea hookeri is popular as a ornamental plant due to its unique appearance and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea hookeri has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, sword-like, and arranged in a compact spiral, with a dark green color and a waxy coating.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, thick base that supports the rosette, with a smooth, grayish-green surface. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by colorful bracts, with purple and white flowers that bloom in the summer and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a berry that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the air, typical of epiphytic growth.
