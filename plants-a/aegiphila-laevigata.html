Aegiphila laevigata

Aegiphila laevigata, commonly known as the smoothleaf aegiphila, is a shrub native to the Caribbean region, particularly found in parts of Cuba and the Bahamas.

It belongs to the Lamiaceae family, which includes many aromatic plants known for their medicinal and culinary uses. The plant is characterized by its hairy, lance-shaped leaves and clusters of small, tubular flowers that range in color from white to pale purple. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in coastal areas or near water sources.

Due to its limited distribution and habitat specificity, it is considered a rare and potentially endangered species.

Morphology

Aegiphila laevigata has leaves that are ovate to elliptic in shape, arranged oppositely along the stem, with a glossy dark green color and a smooth, waxy surface.

The stem is slender, somewhat woody, with a grayish-brown bark that is smooth and slightly fissured. Its flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, with white to pale pink petals, blooming during the dry season and pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapting to the dry, sandy soils of its native habitat.
