Aechmea tricolor

Aechmea tricolor, commonly known as the rainbow bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant produces vibrant, colorful inflorescences that range from red to yellow and orange, adding to its ornamental appeal. Its foliage is usually a mix of green and silver hues, creating a visually appealing contrast.

Aechmea tricolor thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea tricolor has broad, strap-like leaves that are arranged in a rosette pattern, with each leaf measuring up to 30 cm in length and exhibiting a striking combination of green, white, and pink hues.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead forming a compact rosette with a dense, woolly covering on the central rosette that resembles a tuft of hair. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, typically displaying vibrant pink or red colors, and bloom during the summer months, primarily pollinated by insects such as bees and beetles. The fruit is a small, capsule-like structure that contains numerous tiny seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the surrounding environment, typical of epiphytic plants.
