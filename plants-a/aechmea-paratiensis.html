Aechmea paratiensis

Aechmea paratiensis, commonly known as the Paraty bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of South America.

It features a rosette of narrow, grass-like leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater and nutrients. The plant's leaves are typically dark green with a waxy coating, aiding in water retention. It produces vibrant, showy inflorescences with colorful bracts that can range from red to orange.

Aechmea paratiensis is valued for its ornamental appeal and ability to thrive in humid, shaded environments.

Morphology

Aechmea paratiensis has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, strap-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a waxy, bluish-green color and a pointed tip.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, fibrous base that supports the rosette structure. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, with small, white to pink flowers that bloom in the dry season and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a bright red berry that disperses seeds through animal consumption.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in the soil while absorbing moisture and nutrients.
