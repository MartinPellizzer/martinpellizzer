Aconitum ludingense

Aconitum ludingense, commonly known as the Luding Aconite, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of China.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, spurred flowers that range in color from blue to purple. The plant typically grows to a height of 1 to 2 meters, featuring basal leaves with deeply lobed or pinnately divided foliage. Its root system consists of tubers, which are toxic and contain potent alkaloids.

Aconitum ludingense thrives in cool, moist environments and is often found in alpine meadows and rocky habitats.

Morphology

Aconitum ludingense has compound, palmately arranged leaves that are typically deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter green underside.

The stems are erect, smooth, and somewhat hairy, with a greenish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, arranged in dense clusters, and bloom in late summer; they are pollinated primarily by insects. The plant produces follicles as its fruit, containing small, dark seeds that are dispersed by wind.

The roots are tuberous, which help the plant survive in its native alpine environment.
