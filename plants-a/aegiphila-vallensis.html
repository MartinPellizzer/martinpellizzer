Aegiphila vallensis

Aegiphila vallensis, commonly known as the valley aegiphila, is a perennial shrub native to the Andean regions of South America.

It belongs to the family Lamiaceae, which is characterized by square stems and aromatic leaves. The plant typically grows in rocky or mountainous habitats, thriving in well-drained soils. Its small, tubular flowers are usually pink or purple, and it produces small, dry fruits that contain seeds.

Aegiphila vallensis is valued for its ornamental appeal and is sometimes cultivated in gardens for its hardy nature.

Morphology

Aegiphila vallensis has compound, opposite leaves that are ovate to elliptic in shape, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside, typically arranged in pairs along the stem.

The stem is slender, cylindrical, and has a smooth, pale green bark that grows upright with few branches. Its flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, with pale yellow petals and a tubular corolla, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by wind. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapted to absorb moisture from the dry, rocky soil.
