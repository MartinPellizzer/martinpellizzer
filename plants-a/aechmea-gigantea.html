Aechmea gigantea

Aechmea gigantea, commonly known as the giant bromeliad, is a striking plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its large, rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant's foliage is often brightly colored, ranging from green to red, depending on the variety. It produces tall, branched inflorescences that bear showy, long-lasting flowers in shades of white, pink, or red.

Aechmea gigantea thrives in humid, shaded environments and is often used as an ornamental plant in gardens and indoor spaces.

Morphology

Aechmea gigantea has broad, fleshy leaves that are typically sword-shaped, arranged in a rosette pattern, and can grow up to 2 meters in length, with a deep green color and a waxy coating.

The plant has a short, thick stem that is often hidden beneath the leaves, with a rough, fibrous bark. Its flowers are clustered in a dense inflorescence, featuring bright red or orange bracts that enclose small, greenish-white flowers, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds. The fruit is a red, fleshy berry that contains small, black seeds, which are dispersed by birds consuming the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and well-developed, anchoring the plant and absorbing water and nutrients from the soil.
