Actaea vulgaris

Actaea vulgaris, commonly known as white baneberry, is a herbaceous perennial plant native to eastern North America.

It features compound leaves with three to five leaflets and produces clusters of small, white, spiny berries that are toxic to humans and animals. The plant thrives in moist, shaded woodlands and has a short stem with a single flower at the top. Its botanical name reflects its historical use in traditional medicine, though it is not recommended for modern therapeutic purposes.

The plant is notable for its striking appearance and ecological significance in forest ecosystems.

The stem is smooth, slender, and usually reddish-brown, with a growth pattern that is upright and somewhat branched. Its flowers are white to pale green, clustered in an umbel, and bloom in late spring to early summer, being pollinated by insects such as bees. The roots are tuberous and rhizomatous, allowing the plant to spread vegetatively and survive in shaded woodland environments.

Native Distribution

Actaea vulgaris is primarily native to temperate regions of Europe, western Asia, and parts of northern Africa.
