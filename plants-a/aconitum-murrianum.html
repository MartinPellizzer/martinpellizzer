Aconitum murrianum

Aconitum murrianum, commonly known as the Japanese monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Japan.

It features striking, hood-shaped blue or purple flowers that bloom in late summer. The plant has dark green, palmately divided leaves that are deeply lobed and resemble the shape of a hand. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it should be handled with care and is often grown in botanical gardens for its ornamental value.

Morphology

Aconitum murrianum has compound, palmately arranged leaves that are deeply lobed, typically green with a slightly hairy texture, and can reach up to 15 cm in length.

The stem is upright, hollow, and covered with a smooth, dark green to brown bark, growing in a clump-forming pattern. Its flowers are blue to purple, bell-shaped, and arranged in dense clusters, blooming in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a follicle that releases seeds through wind dispersal, and the seeds are small, winged, and light.

The roots are tuberous, fibrous, and capable of storing nutrients for the plant's survival.
