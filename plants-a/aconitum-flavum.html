Aconitum flavum

Aconitum flavum, commonly known as the yellow aconite, is a herbaceous perennial plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It features striking, large, yellow to orange flowers that bloom in late summer, typically from July to September. The plant has deeply lobed, palmately divided leaves that are dark green and velvety in texture. It thrives in well-drained, moist soils and prefers partial to full shade in its natural habitat.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it is important to handle the plant with care and avoid ingestion.

The stem is upright, hollow, and has a smooth, grayish-green bark that becomes rougher with age. Its flowers are large, showy, and blue to violet in color, arranged in racemes, blooming from late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and fibrous, adapted for absorbing moisture from the moist, cool environments where the plant thrives.

Native Distribution

Aconitum flavum is primarily native to China North-Central, China South-Central, Inner Mongolia, Qinghai, and Tibet.

This plant is commonly found in high-altitude regions within these areas. It typically grows in mountainous and alpine environments. The species is well adapted to the cold and rocky conditions of its native habitat.

It plays an important ecological role in these regions.
