Aechmea salzmannii

Aechmea salzmannii, commonly known as the Salzmann's bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to Central and South America.

It features a rosette of narrow, grass-like leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients. The plant's foliage is often brightly colored, with shades of red, orange, and yellow, making it visually appealing. It produces inflorescences with showy, long-lasting flowers that range from white to pink.

Aechmea salzmannii thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular as a decorative houseplant.

Morphology

Aechmea salzmannii has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, stiff, and covered with a dense layer of white to grayish trichomes, giving them a fuzzy appearance.

The leaves are arranged in a compact rosette, with each leaf measuring up to 30 cm in length and 5 cm in width, and they are usually a deep green color. The plant has a short, stocky stem that is covered with a fibrous, rough bark, and it grows upright with a compact growth pattern. Its flowers are clustered in a terminal inflorescence, consisting of showy, violet to purple bracts that enclose small, greenish-white flowers, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by insects.

The fruit is a bright red, fleshy capsule that disperses seeds through animal consumption and wind.
