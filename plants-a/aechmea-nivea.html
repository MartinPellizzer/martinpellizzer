Aechmea nivea

Aechmea nivea, commonly known as the silver vane bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater and nutrients. The leaves are typically covered in a dense layer of white, woolly hair, giving the plant its distinctive appearance. It produces showy inflorescences with bright red flowers that bloom for several months.

Aechmea nivea thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular as a decorative plant in tropical and subtropical gardens.

Morphology

Aechmea nivea has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, arranged in a tight spiral, with a waxy, silver-green color and a sharp, pointed tip.

The plant lacks a true stem, with the base of the leaves forming a dense, compact rosette that can sometimes have a slightly fibrous texture. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, with small, tubular flowers that bloom in late summer to early fall, pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a berry that turns red when mature, dispersing seeds through bird consumption and dropping.

The roots are fibrous and adapted to absorb moisture from the surrounding environment.
