Adiantum viscosum

Adiantum viscosum, commonly known as the staghorn fern, is a unique epiphytic fern that thrives in humid, tropical environments.

It is characterized by its distinctive, tree-like appearance with a central stem and overlapping, lobed fronds that resemble antlers, hence its common name. The plant has a symbiotic relationship with specific fungi, which aid in nutrient absorption from the air and rain. Its fronds are covered in a fine, sticky substance that helps retain moisture and protect against pests.

Adiantum viscosum is often cultivated for its ornamental value and is well-suited for hanging baskets or mounting on bark.

Morphology

Adiantum viscosum has fern-like leaves that are pinnately divided, with a delicate, lace-like appearance, typically ranging from 10 to 30 cm in length.

The leaves are dark green on top and lighter underneath, and they are arranged alternately along the stem. The stem is slender and upright, with a smooth, pale green bark that grows in a compact, clumping form.

It produces small, inconspicuous greenish-white flowers in early spring, which are wind-pollinated. The fruit is a capsule that releases tiny, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and help anchor the plant in moist, shaded environments.
