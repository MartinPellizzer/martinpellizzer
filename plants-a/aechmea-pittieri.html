Aechmea pittieri

Aechmea pittieri, commonly known as the velvet bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater to support its growth. The leaves are thick, waxy, and often display a deep purple or reddish hue, contributing to its ornamental appeal. It produces striking inflorescences with bright red or orange flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette.

This plant thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular in horticulture for its unique appearance and ease of care.

Morphology

Aechmea pittieri has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, fleshy, and often have a bluish-gray waxy coating, arranged in a compact rosette at the base of the plant.

The leaves are typically long, reaching up to 60 cm in length, with a pointed tip and a smooth, leathery texture. The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, often hidden stem with a thick, fibrous, and sometimes spongy bark. Its inflorescence is a spadix surrounded by colorful bracts, with small, white or pale pink flowers that bloom in the dry season and are pollinated by insects.

The plant produces small, berry-like fruits that are dispersed by birds and animals.
