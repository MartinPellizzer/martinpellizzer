Aegiphila conturbata

Aegiphila conturbata, commonly known as the roughleaf aegiphila, is a perennial shrub native to the highland regions of South America.

It belongs to the Lamiaceae family, which includes many aromatic herbs and shrubs. The plant is characterized by its hairy, lance-shaped leaves and clusters of small, tubular flowers that range in color from white to purple. It thrives in well-drained soils and is often found in rocky or mountainous habitats.

Due to its unique morphology and habitat, it is of interest to botanists and conservationists.

Morphology

Aegiphila conturbata has leaves that are elliptic to ovate in shape, alternate in arrangement, and range from 2 to 5 centimeters in length, with a dark green, glossy upper surface and a lighter, sometimes hairy underside.

The stem is slender and somewhat woody, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that becomes fissured with age, and it grows in a sprawling or upright manner depending on environmental conditions. The flowers are small, clustered in inflorescences, with pinkish-white petals and yellow stamens, blooming from late spring to early summer, and are primarily pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in sandy or rocky soils.
