Aechmea matudae

Aechmea matudae, commonly known as the Matudae bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of Central America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaf structure that forms a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant's leaves are typically dark green with a waxy coating, and they can grow up to 1 meter in length. Aechmea matudae produces vibrant red or orange flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette, adding a dramatic visual contrast.

It thrives in humid, shaded environments and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea matudae has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, sword-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a dark green color and a waxy, leathery texture.

The stem is short and thick, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark, and it grows upright from the base of the rosette. The plant produces striking inflorescences with colorful bracts, featuring tubular flowers in shades of red, orange, and yellow, which bloom during the dry season and are pollinated by birds and insects. The fruit is a red, fleshy berry that contains small, black seeds, which are dispersed by animals eating the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and form a dense network around the base of the plant, helping to anchor it and absorb moisture from the surrounding soil.
