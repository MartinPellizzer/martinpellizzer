Aechmea mitis

Aechmea mitis, commonly known as the silver vanda, is a bromeliad native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater for the plant's use. The leaves are typically broad, stiff, and covered in a silvery or white powdery coating, giving the plant its common name. It produces striking, long-lasting inflorescences with vibrant pink or red flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette.

This plant is often cultivated as an ornamental due to its attractive appearance and relatively easy care requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea mitis has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, strap-like, and arranged in a spiral around the central stem, with a waxy, bluish-green color and a leathery texture.

The stem is short and thick, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that grows upright from the base of the plant. The plant produces inflorescences with spadices and bracts, bearing small, pale pink to white flowers that bloom in the summer, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a capsule that releases seeds through mechanical means, and the seeds are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in its epiphytic or terrestrial habitat.
