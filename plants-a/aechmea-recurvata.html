Aechmea recurvata

Aechmea recurvata, commonly known as the recurved pineapple plant, is a bromeliad native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater for the plant's use. The plant produces striking, spadix-like inflorescences that are often brightly colored, typically in shades of red, orange, or yellow. Its leaves are thick and waxy, adapted to retain moisture in its natural habitat.

Aechmea recurvata is often cultivated as an ornamental plant due to its attractive appearance and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea recurvata has rosette-shaped leaves that are typically sword-like, arranged in a tight spiral around the central stem, with a dark green to bluish-gray color and a waxy coating.

The plant has a short, thick stem that is often hidden beneath the leaves, with a rough, fibrous texture and a pale green to reddish-brown color. Its inflorescence consists of a tall, spiky flower spike bearing white or pale blue flowers, which bloom during the summer months and are pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds. The fruit is a small, berry-like structure that turns red when mature, dispersing seeds through animal consumption and wind.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in its rocky or sandy habitat.
