Aconitum vilmorianum

Aconitum vilmorianum, commonly known as the Vilmoriana monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, spurred blue or purple flowers that resemble a hood, giving it its common name. The plant has palmately lobed leaves and produces toxic alkaloids, making it both fascinating and dangerous. It thrives in cool, moist environments and is often found in meadows and rocky slopes at high altitudes.

Due to its ornamental value and ecological significance, it is sometimes cultivated in gardens, though care must be taken due to its poisonous nature.

Morphology

Aconitum vilmorianum has leaves that are typically compound, with a palmate or deeply lobed shape, arranged alternately along the stem, and characterized by their dark green color and coarse texture.

The stem is usually erect and somewhat hairy, with a greenish-brown bark that becomes rougher as the plant matures. Its flowers are showy, arranged in racemes, featuring blue or purple petals and a spurred shape, blooming in late summer, and are pollinated primarily by insects such as bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, often used in traditional medicine for their potent alkaloid content.
