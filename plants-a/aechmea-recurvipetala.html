Aechmea recurvipetala

Aechmea recurvipetala, commonly known as the recurving pineapple bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that are often covered in a dense layer of trichomes, which help in water retention. The plant produces a striking inflorescence with vivid red bracts that enclose small, white flowers. Its leaves are typically long, narrow, and recurved, giving it a unique, almost spiky appearance.

Aechmea recurvipetala thrives in humid, shaded environments and is often cultivated as an ornamental plant for its aesthetic appeal.

Morphology

Aechmea recurvipetala has leaves that are broad, strap-shaped, and arranged in a rosette, with a waxy, dark green surface and a bluish-white underside.

The plant has a short, thick stem with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that grows upright. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, with purple and yellow hues, blooming in late summer and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small, fleshy capsule that disperses seeds by wind and animal consumption.

The roots are fibrous and form a dense network near the soil surface, anchoring the plant and absorbing nutrients.
