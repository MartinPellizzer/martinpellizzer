Aechmea poeppigii

Aechmea poeppigii, commonly known as the Christmas chime, is a striking bromeliad native to Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central tank to collect rainwater, a feature common among bromeliads. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences that range in color from red to orange, adding ornamental value. Its leaves are typically broad and strap-like, with a waxy surface that helps reduce water loss.

Aechmea poeppigii thrives in bright, indirect light and is popular as a houseplant due to its low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea poeppigii has rosette-shaped leaves that are wide, fleshy, and often have a bluish or grayish hue due to a dense layer of trichomes.

The leaves are arranged in a compact rosette, with each leaf measuring up to 30 cm in length and 10 cm in width. The plant has a short, thick stem that is often hidden beneath the leaves, with a smooth, grayish bark. Its flowers are borne in a terminal inflorescence, consisting of showy, violet to purple bracts that enclose small white to pale blue flowers, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by wind or insects.

The fruit is a capsule that releases small, dark brown seeds, which are dispersed by wind or water.
