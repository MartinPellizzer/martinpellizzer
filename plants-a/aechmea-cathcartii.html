Aechmea cathcartii

Aechmea cathcartii, commonly known as the Cathcart's bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of Central America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant produces striking inflorescences with vibrant purple flowers that bloom in late spring or early summer. Its foliage is often covered in a dense layer of trichomes, which help in water retention and gas exchange.

Aechmea cathcartii is appreciated for its ornamental value and is often cultivated as a houseplant due to its adaptability and aesthetic appeal.

Morphology

Aechmea cathcartii has rosette-shaped leaves that are typically broad and sword-like, arranged in a tight spiral at the base of the plant, with a waxy, dark green surface that can develop a bluish tint.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead forming a compact rosette with a dense, spongy central structure that resembles a pseudostem. Its flowers are borne in a spike at the center of the rosette, consisting of bright red or orange bracts that enclose small, greenish-white flowers, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by wind or insects. The fruit is a small, fleshy capsule that produces numerous seeds, which are dispersed by wind or water.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the surrounding soil, typical of epiphytic bromeliads.
