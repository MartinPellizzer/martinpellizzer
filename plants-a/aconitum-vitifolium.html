Aconitum vitifolium

Aconitum vitifolium, commonly known as the grape-leaved aconite, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stem and large, palmately compound leaves that resemble grape leaves, hence its common name. The plant produces striking blue to purple flowers in late summer, arranged in dense, terminal clusters. It thrives in cool, moist environments and is often found in rocky or woodland soils.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it is considered a potentially dangerous plant for both humans and animals.

Aconitum vitifolium has compound, palmately arranged leaves that are large, deeply lobed, and have a dark green upper surface with a silvery underside.

The stems are tall, hairy, and have a greenish-brown bark that becomes rough with age. Its flowers are blue to violet, arranged in racemes, and bloom in late summer; they are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a follicle containing small, winged seeds that are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, tuberous, and store nutrients for the plant.

Native Distribution

Aconitum vitifolium is primarily native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia, particularly in countries such as Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, and Tajikistan.

It thrives in alpine environments and rocky slopes at high altitudes. This species is commonly found in the Pamir and Tien Shan mountain ranges. It prefers well-drained soils and is adapted to the cold, harsh climate of its native habitat.

Aconitum vitifolium is often associated with meadows and grasslands in these elevated regions.
