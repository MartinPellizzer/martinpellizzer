Aconitum sturiense

Aconitum sturiense, commonly known as the Sturiense monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, hood-shaped blue to purple flowers. The plant has dark green, palmately lobed leaves that are often covered in a waxy coating. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Aconitum sturiense is known for its toxic alkaloids, making it a plant that requires careful handling.

Morphology

Aconitum sturiense has compound, palmately arranged leaves that are large, broadly ovate, and often deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes hairy underside.

The stem is smooth and cylindrical, typically greenish-brown in color, with a straight growth pattern and no significant branching. Its flowers are showy, blue to violet in color, with five petals and a spurs-like structure, blooming in late summer; they are pollinated primarily by insects such as bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, flat seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, adapted for storage and anchoring the plant in its habitat.
