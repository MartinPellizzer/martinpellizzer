Aellenia auricula

Aellenia auricula, commonly known as the golden cupplant, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to parts of Asia and Africa.

It is characterized by its basal rosette of broad, heart-shaped leaves that are often covered in fine hairs. The plant produces showy yellow flowers arranged in a cup-like inflorescence, which gives it its common name. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in wetlands or along stream banks.

Aellenia auricula is valued for its ornamental appeal and has traditional uses in some cultures.

Morphology

Aellenia auricula has leaves that are ovate to elliptical in shape, with a glossy dark green upper surface and a lighter underside, arranged alternately along the stem.

The stem is slender, with a smooth, pale green bark that becomes rougher with age, and it grows upright with a branching pattern. The flowers are small, clustered in umbels, with white petals and yellow stamens, blooming in late spring to early summer, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in moist, well-drained soils.
