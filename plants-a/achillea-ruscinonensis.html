Achillea ruscinonensis

Achillea ruscinonensis, commonly known as the Ruscinonese yarrow, is a perennial herb native to the Balkan Peninsula.

It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is characterized by its gray-green, aromatic foliage and flat, composite flower heads. The plant typically grows in dry, rocky soils and is often found in mountainous regions. Its flowers bloom in late spring to early summer, producing a range of colors from white to pale pink.

Achillea ruscinonensis is valued for its ornamental appeal and has historical uses in traditional medicine.

Morphology

Achillea ruscinonensis has leaves that are lanceolate in shape, alternate in arrangement, and typically range from 5 to 15 cm in length, with a gray-green color and a fuzzy texture.

The stem is erect, hairy, and has a light green to reddish-brown bark, growing in a compact, bushy pattern. Its flowers are composite, consisting of yellow disk flowers surrounded by white to pale pink ray florets, blooming from June to August, and are primarily pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a small, dry achene with a pappus for wind dispersal, allowing the seeds to travel considerable distances.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, aiding in the plant's ability to spread and colonize new areas.
