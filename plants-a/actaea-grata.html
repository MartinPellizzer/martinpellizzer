Actaea grata

Actaea grata, commonly known as the showy lady's mantle, is a perennial plant native to eastern North America.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, palmately lobed leaves that are often covered in fine hairs. The plant produces clusters of white, star-shaped flowers in early summer, which are followed by red, fuzzy berries. Actaea grata thrives in moist, shaded woodlands and is valued for its ornamental beauty and ecological importance.

It is also noted for its medicinal uses in traditional herbal practices.

Morphology

Actaea grata has compound, pinnately arranged leaves that are usually ovate to elliptical in shape, with a glossy green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes reddish underside.

The stems are slender, with a smooth, reddish-brown bark that becomes more furrowed with age, and they grow upright with a somewhat branched pattern. The flowers are small, white to cream-colored, clustered in upright cymes, and are pollinated by insects such as bees; they bloom in late spring to early summer. The fruit is a red, berry-like aggregate, containing numerous small seeds, which are dispersed by birds and other animals.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, helping the plant to store nutrients and survive in various soil conditions.
