Aechmea polyantha

Aechmea polyantha, commonly known as the pineapple bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaf structure, which forms a central cup that collects water and nutrients. The leaves are typically broad, stiff, and covered in a waxy coating, aiding in water retention. It produces vibrant, showy inflorescences with colorful bracts that can range from red to orange.

This plant is popular in horticulture for its ornamental value and ability to thrive in a variety of indoor environments.

Morphology

Aechmea polyantha has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, strap-like, and arranged in a spiral around the central rosette, with a waxy, bluish-green color and a pointed tip.

The plant lacks a true stem, but it has a short, fibrous root system that anchors it to the substrate. It produces vibrant red or orange flower spikes that emerge from the center of the rosette, with small, tubular flowers that bloom during the summer months and are pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds. The fruit is a three-lobed, fleshy berry that turns red when mature, and the seeds are dispersed by wind and animals.

The plant's morphology is well-adapted for epiphytic growth, with thick, water-storing leaves that help it thrive in humid, shaded environments.
