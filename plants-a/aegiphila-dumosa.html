Aegiphila dumosa

Aegiphila dumosa, commonly known as the hairy false thorn, is a shrub native to the arid regions of the southwestern United States and northern Mexico.

It is characterized by its small, gray-green leaves that are covered in fine hairs, which help reduce water loss in its harsh environment. The plant produces clusters of small, yellowish flowers during the spring and summer months. Its root system is extensive, allowing it to anchor itself in sandy or rocky soils.

Aegiphila dumosa is well adapted to drought conditions and is often found in desert scrub and chaparral habitats.

Morphology

Aegiphila dumosa has small, ovate leaves that are arranged alternately along the stem, with a bluish-green color and a rough, hairy texture.

The stem is erect, woody, and has a rough, grayish-brown bark, growing in a branching pattern. Its flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, with white to pale pink petals, blooming in late winter to early spring, and are pollinated by wind. The fruit is a dry, capsule that disperses seeds by wind, with small, light seeds that are easily carried by the air.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, helping the plant anchor in rocky or dry soils.
