Aegiphila diffusa

Aegiphila diffusa, commonly known as the Brazilian bush or velvetleaf, is a shrub native to South America.

It is characterized by its aromatic, hairy leaves and small, clustered white flowers. The plant typically grows in tropical and subtropical regions, often in shaded or moist environments. Its leaves are used in traditional medicine for their purported anti-inflammatory properties.

Aegiphila diffusa is also valued in horticulture for its ornamental appeal and adaptability to various soil types.

Morphology

Aegiphila diffusa has small, oval-shaped leaves that are arranged oppositely along the stem, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is slender, hairy, and has a reddish-brown bark that peels in thin layers. Its flowers are clustered in umbels, featuring white to pale pink petals and yellow stamens, blooming in spring and being pollinated by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that releases small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, helping the plant anchor in rocky or sandy soils.
