Aconitum martjanovii

Aconitum martjanovii, commonly known as the Siberian monkshood, is a perennial herb native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking blue or violet spurred flowers. The plant has large, deeply lobed leaves that are often covered in a fine, waxy coating. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in alpine or subalpine habitats.

Aconitum martjanovii contains potent toxins, making it both a dangerous and notable species in botanical studies.

Morphology

Aconitum martjanovii has leaves that are compound, with palmately arranged leaflets that are ovate to lanceolate in shape, dark green in color, and often slightly hairy.

The stem is erect, smooth, and glabrous, with a reddish-brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are blue to purple, forming dense panicle inflorescences, and bloom in late summer; they are pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a follicle that releases small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, tuberous, and extensively branched, serving as storage organs for nutrients.
