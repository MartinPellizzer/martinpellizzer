Aegiphila tetragona

Aegiphila tetragona, commonly known as the four-angled shrub, is a perennial plant native to the Mediterranean region.

It is characterized by its upright, branching structure and small, aromatic leaves that are often four-angled in cross-section. The plant produces small, tubular flowers that are typically pink or purple, blooming in late spring to early summer. It thrives in well-drained soils and full sun, making it suitable for rock gardens and dry landscapes.

Aegiphila tetragona is valued for its ornamental appeal and drought tolerance.

Morphology

Aegiphila tetragona has leaves that are lance-shaped, arranged oppositely along the stem, and typically range from 2 to 5 centimeters in length, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is upright, has a smooth texture, and is reddish-brown in color, growing in a bushy pattern. Its flowers are small, clustered in terminal inflorescences, with tubular structures and a pale yellow to greenish color, blooming from late spring to early summer, and are primarily pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small, dry capsule that releases tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapting to the dry conditions of its native habitat.
