Aechmea penduliflora

Aechmea penduliflora, commonly known as the pendant bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant produces long, pendulous inflorescences that bear showy, colorful flowers, typically in shades of red, orange, or yellow. Its foliage is often covered in a dense layer of trichomes, which help in water retention and protection from intense sunlight.

Aechmea penduliflora is popular as a ornamental plant due to its attractive appearance and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea penduliflora has rosette-shaped leaves that are long, narrow, and sword-like, arranged in a tight spiral at the base of the plant, with a waxy, dark green color and a pointed tip.

The stem is short and thick, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that grows upright from the base. Its flowers are clustered in a panicle inflorescence, featuring tubular white petals with yellow tips, blooming during the summer months, and are pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds. The plant produces berry-like fruits that are red when ripe, containing small seeds dispersed by birds and wind.

Its roots are fibrous and form a dense network near the soil surface, helping to anchor the plant and absorb nutrients.
