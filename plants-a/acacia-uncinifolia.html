Acacia uncinifolia

Acacia uncinifolia, commonly known as the hook-leaved acacia, is a shrub native to southern Africa.

It is characterized by its compound leaves, which are often covered in a fine layer of white, waxy hairs. The plant produces yellow flowers arranged in cylindrical inflorescences, which are highly attractive to pollinators. Its seeds are enclosed in pod-like structures that split open to release the seeds.

Acacia uncinifolia is adapted to arid environments and is often found in savannas and rocky areas.

Native Distribution

Acacia uncinifolia is primarily native to the arid and semi-arid regions of southern Africa, including countries such as Namibia, Botswana, and South Africa.

It thrives in sandy soils and is commonly found in savannas and shrublands. This species is well adapted to drought conditions and can tolerate high temperatures. It plays an important role in local ecosystems by providing habitat and food for various wildlife species.

Acacia uncinifolia is also sometimes cultivated for its ornamental value and for its use in traditional medicine.

Introduced Distribution

Acacia uncinifolia was mainly introduced to South Africa as a potential source of timber and for its ability to stabilize soil in arid regions.

It has since spread beyond its original introduction sites, becoming naturalized in several provinces. The plant is commonly found in savanna and grassland ecosystems, where it forms dense thickets. Its rapid growth and adaptability have led to concerns about its invasive potential in some areas.
