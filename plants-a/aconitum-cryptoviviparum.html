Aconitum cryptoviviparum

Aconitum cryptoviviparum, commonly known as the cryptoviviparous monkshood, is a perennial herb native to the alpine regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stem and large, showy blue to violet flowers. The plant is notable for its unique reproductive strategy, as it produces viable seeds while still enclosed within the fruit, a trait known as cryptovivipary. It thrives in cool, moist environments and is often found in rocky or mountainous terrain.

Due to its limited distribution and specific habitat requirements, it is considered a rare and specialized species.

Morphology

Aconitum cryptoviviparum has dark green, ovate to lanceolate leaves that are arranged alternately along the stem, with a rough, hairy texture and a bluish-green underside.

The stem is erect, hairy, and has a grayish-brown bark that becomes rough with age, growing in a single, upright pattern. The flowers are showy, blue to purple, spurred flowers with five sepals and two petals, blooming in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated primarily by bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, allowing the plant to spread vegetatively.
