Arceuthobium vaginatum var. cryptopodum

Arceuthobium vaginatum var. cryptopodum, commonly known as the Indian pine or witch's needle, is a parasitic plant that relies entirely on host plants for its survival.

It lacks chlorophyll and cannot perform photosynthesis, making it entirely dependent on its host for nutrients. The plant is characterized by its small, needle-like leaves and its unique method of seed dispersal, where the seeds are catapulted from the host plant. It typically infects plants in the Ericaceae family, such as blueberries and huckleberries.

Due to its parasitic nature and limited growth, it plays a minor role in ecosystem dynamics but remains an intriguing subject in botanical studies.
