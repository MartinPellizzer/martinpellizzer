Aechmea weilbachii

Aechmea weilbachii, commonly known as the velvet plant, is a striking bromeliad native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped foliage, which forms a central cup that collects rainwater. The leaves are thick, fleshy, and often have a velvety texture, giving the plant its common name. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences in shades of red, orange, and yellow.

It thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea weilbachii has rosette-shaped leaves that are stiff, grass-like, and arranged in a compact spiral, with a waxy blue-green color and a pointed tip.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, thick base that supports the rosette, with a smooth, pale green surface. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a spathe, with violet to purple flowers that bloom in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a three-lobed capsule that releases seeds through mechanical means when mature.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring in the soil, with some rhizomes contributing to vegetative propagation.
