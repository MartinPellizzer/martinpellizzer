Aechmea rubrolilacina

Aechmea rubrolilacina, commonly known as the pink-lavender bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant produces vibrant, showy inflorescences with flowers in shades of pink and lavender, which bloom for several months. Its foliage is often covered in a fine, white powdery coating known as trichomes, which help in water absorption and protection from intense sunlight.

Aechmea rubrolilacina thrives in warm, humid environments and is popular as a decorative plant in both indoor and outdoor settings.

Morphology

Aechmea rubrolilacina has rosette-shaped leaves that are stiff, sword-like, and dark green with a reddish-purple margin, arranged in a dense spiral around the central rosette.

The plant lacks a true stem, with the base of the leaves forming a compact, leafy rosette that holds water. Its flowers are clustered in a dense inflorescence, with bright pink to purple petals and yellow stamens, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds. The fruit is a fleshy, red berry that contains small, black seeds, which are dispersed by animals eating the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing water from the surrounding soil, typical of epiphytic bromeliads.
