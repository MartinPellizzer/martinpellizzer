Aconitum aradanicum

Aconitum aradanicum, commonly known as the Armenian monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the Caucasus region.

It features striking purple to blue flowers arranged in a spire-like inflorescence, which bloom in late summer. The plant has large, heart-shaped leaves that are deeply veined and covered in a soft, hairy texture. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it should be handled with care and is often cultivated in botanical gardens for its ornamental value.

Morphology

Aconitum aradanicum has large, compound leaves that are deeply lobed, arranged alternately along the stem, and typically measure up to 30 cm in length, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is upright, smooth, and typically has a reddish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, with five petals and a spurred shape, blooming in summer and pollinated primarily by bees. The plant produces follicles as its fruit, containing small, black seeds that are dispersed by wind.

Its roots are thick, fleshy, and extensively branched, helping it to store nutrients and survive in poor soil conditions.
