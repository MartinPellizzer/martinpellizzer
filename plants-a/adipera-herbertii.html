Adipera herbertii

Adipera herbertii, commonly known as Herbert's beardtongue, is a perennial flowering plant native to the western United States.

It belongs to the plantain family, Plantaginaceae, and is characterized by its hairy, upright stems and clusters of pink to purple flowers. The leaves are lance-shaped and often covered in fine hairs, which help reduce water loss in arid environments. It thrives in well-drained soils and full sun, making it a popular choice for xeriscaping.

The plant's unique appearance and adaptability to harsh conditions make it both ecologically and horticulturally significant.

Morphology

Adipera herbertii has leaves that are narrow, lance-shaped, and arranged alternately along the stem, with a bluish-green color and a slightly hairy surface.

The stem is slender, with a smooth, greenish-brown bark that becomes more fibrous with age, and it grows in a climbing or trailing pattern. Its flowers are small, clustered in inflorescences, with white petals and yellow stamens, blooming in early spring and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, allowing the plant to anchor itself in rocky or sandy soils.
