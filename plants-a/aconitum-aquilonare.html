Aconitum aquilonare

Aconitum aquilonare, commonly known as the Alpine monkshood, is a herbaceous perennial native to the mountainous regions of Europe.

It is characterized by its striking violet-blue flowers that bloom in late summer, typically from July to August. The plant features palmately lobed leaves that are deeply divided, giving it a distinctive appearance. It thrives in well-drained, loamy soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Aconitum aquilonare is a member of the Ranunculaceae family, known for its toxic alkaloids, making it a plant that requires careful handling.

Morphology

Aconitum aquilonare has compound, palmately lobed leaves that are typically dark green on top and lighter underneath, with a coarse texture and a serrated edge.

The stem is upright, hairy, and has a grayish-brown bark that becomes smoother as the plant matures. Its flowers are blue to purple, bell-shaped, and arranged in racemes, blooming in late summer and attracting pollinators like bees through nectar. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are deep and fibrous, helping the plant anchor itself and absorb nutrients from the soil.
