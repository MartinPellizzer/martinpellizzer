Aegiphila pavoniana

Aegiphila pavoniana, commonly known as the velvetleaf aegiphila, is a shrub native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its dense, velvety foliage that gives it its common name. The plant produces small, clustered flowers that range in color from white to pale purple, typically blooming in the spring. Its leaves are often aromatic and have been used in traditional medicine for their purported healing properties.

Aegiphila pavoniana thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in forest understories or along stream banks.

Morphology

Aegiphila pavoniana has narrow, lanceolate leaves that are arranged oppositely along the stem, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is slender and somewhat woody, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its small, clustered flowers are pinkish-white in color, with tubular structures and five petals, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapted to absorb moisture from the dry, rocky soils where the plant typically grows.
