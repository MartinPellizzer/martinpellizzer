Aegochloa atractyloides

Aegochloa atractyloides, commonly known as purple false brome, is a perennial grass native to the western United States.

It is characterized by its tufted growth habit and bluish-green foliage that turns a striking purple in the fall. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences that bear tiny, wind-pollinated flowers. It thrives in well-drained soils and is often found in open grasslands and along stream banks.

Due to its ornamental value and ecological benefits, it is sometimes used in restoration projects.

Morphology

Aegochloa atractyloides has narrow, linear leaves that are typically 5–15 cm long and 2–4 mm wide, arranged in a dense, grass-like rosette with a bluish-green color.

The stem is cylindrical, smooth, and slightly hairy, with a light green to pale yellowish color, and grows in a spreading or clump-forming pattern. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences with pale green to brown flowers that bloom from late spring to early summer, and are wind-pollinated. The fruit is a small, dry, three-angled achene, dispersed by wind, and the seeds are lightweight with a pappus for aid in wind dispersal.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, helping the plant to spread and establish itself in wetland or marshy environments.
