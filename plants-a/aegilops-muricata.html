Aegilops muricata

Aegilops muricata, commonly known as the rough wheatgrass, is a perennial grass species native to the Mediterranean region.

It is characterized by its tufted growth habit and slender, wiry stems covered in coarse hairs. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences with pale green to yellowish flowers. Its leaves are narrow, rough to the touch, and have a distinctive hairy texture.

Aegilops muricata is often found in dry, rocky habitats and is valued for its ornamental appeal and potential in ecological restoration.

Morphology

Aegilops muricata has narrow, elongated leaves that are grass-like in appearance, with a bluish-green color and a slightly hairy texture, arranged alternately along the stem.

The stem is cylindrical, with a rough, ridged bark that is typically brown in color, and it grows upright with a spreading growth pattern. The plant produces small, greenish-white flowers in dense spikes, blooming in the spring, and they are wind-pollinated. The fruit is a three-lobed, triangular capsule that disperses its seeds by wind when it splits open.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, helping the plant to anchor itself and absorb nutrients from the soil.
