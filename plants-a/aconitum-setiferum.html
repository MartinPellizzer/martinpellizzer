Aconitum setiferum

Aconitum setiferum, commonly known as the alpine monkshood, is a member of the Ranunculaceae family.

It is native to the mountainous regions of Europe and Asia, where it thrives in cool, moist environments. The plant is characterized by its tall, spindly stems and striking, hood-shaped flowers that are typically deep purple or blue. Its leaves are palmately compound, with a distinctive veining pattern that resembles a spider's web.

Aconitum setiferum contains toxic alkaloids, making it potentially dangerous to handle or ingest.

Morphology

Aconitum setiferum has compound, bipinnate leaves that are dark green on top and pale underneath, arranged alternately along the stem, with each leaflet being narrow and lance-shaped.

The stem is upright, smooth, and greenish-blue, growing in a straight, unbranched pattern. The flowers are blue to purple, forming dense clusters at the top of the stem, with a spurred structure and are pollinated by bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, adapted for storing nutrients.
