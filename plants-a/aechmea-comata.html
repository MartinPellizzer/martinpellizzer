Aechmea comata

Aechmea comata, commonly known as the woolly pineapple plant, is a bromeliad native to Mexico and Central America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped foliage that forms a central cup to collect rainwater. The leaves are thick, leathery, and covered in a dense, woolly coating that gives the plant its common name. It produces striking red or orange flowers that emerge from the center of the rosette.

This plant is often cultivated as an ornamental due to its unique appearance and ability to thrive in shaded, humid environments.

Morphology

Aechmea comata has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, strap-like, and dark green with a silvery underside, arranged in a compact spiral at the base.

The plant lacks a true stem, instead having a short, thick, and fibrous base that resembles a pseudostem. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, with pink to red flowers that bloom during the dry season and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a red, fleshy berry containing small seeds, which are dispersed by birds and mammals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in its bromeliad-like rosette structure.
