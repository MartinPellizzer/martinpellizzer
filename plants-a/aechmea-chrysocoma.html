Aechmea chrysocoma

Aechmea chrysocoma, commonly known as the golden rosette bromeliad, is a flowering plant native to Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater for the plant's use. The leaves are typically bright green with a waxy coating, and the plant produces striking inflorescences with vibrant yellow bracts. It thrives in humid, shaded environments and is often found in tropical and subtropical regions.

Aechmea chrysocoma is popular as a houseplant due to its ornamental value and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea chrysocoma has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, stiff, and covered in a dense layer of white trichomes, giving them a silvery appearance.

The leaves are arranged in a tight spiral around the central stem, which is short and often hidden beneath the foliage. The plant produces striking inflorescences with brightly colored flowers that are typically red or orange, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by birds. The fruit is a small, fleshy capsule that disperses seeds through wind and animal consumption.

Its roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring in rocky or acidic soils.
