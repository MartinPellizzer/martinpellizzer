Aconitum algoviense

Aconitum algoviense, commonly known as the Algoviense Monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the high altitudes of the Eastern Carpathians in Romania.

It is characterized by its distinctive hood-shaped flowers, which are typically deep blue or purple in color. The plant has palmately divided leaves that are deeply lobed, contributing to its ornamental appeal. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Aconitum algoviense is also noted for its toxic alkaloids, making it a plant that requires caution when handling.

Morphology

Aconitum algoviense has compound leaves that are doubly pinnate, with finely divided leaflets arranged alternately along the stem, giving the plant a delicate, fern-like appearance.

The leaves are typically green, with a slightly hairy surface, and can reach lengths of up to 30 centimeters. The stem is erect, often hairy, and has a smooth, grayish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age. The flowers are blue to purple, bell-shaped, and arranged in a spicate inflorescence, blooming in late summer; they are pollinated primarily by bees.

The fruit is a follicle that releases seeds through dehiscence, and the seeds are small, smooth, and dispersed by wind.
