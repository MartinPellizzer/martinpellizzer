Aegiphila velutinosa

Aegiphila velutinosa, commonly known as velvetleaf aegiphila, is a flowering plant native to the Caribbean and parts of Central America.

It is characterized by its hairy, velvety leaves that give it its common name. The plant produces small, clustered flowers that are typically white or pale pink in color. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in forest understories.

Aegiphila velutinosa is valued for its ornamental appeal and has some traditional uses in local medicine.

Morphology

Aegiphila velutinosa has leaves that are ovate to elliptic in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, with a velvety texture and a dark green color.

The stem is slender, often hairy, and exhibits a reddish-brown color, growing in a compact, shrub-like form. Its flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, with white to pale pink petals, blooming in the summer months and pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapted for anchoring the plant in rocky or sandy soils.
