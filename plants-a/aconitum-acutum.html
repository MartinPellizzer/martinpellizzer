Aconitum acutum

Aconitum acutum, commonly known as the acute aconite, is a herbaceous perennial native to the mountainous regions of Europe and Asia.

It is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stem that can reach heights of up to 2 meters. The plant produces showy, blue to purple flowers in late summer, which are clustered in racemes at the top of the stem. Its leaves are palmately lobed and have a rough, hairy texture.

Aconitum acutum contains potent alkaloids, making it highly toxic and requiring careful handling.

Morphology

Aconitum acutum has compound, palmately arranged leaves that are typically heart-shaped or ovate, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside, reaching up to 15 cm in length.

The stem is smooth and cylindrical, often reddish-brown in color, with a straight growth pattern and a slightly hairy texture. Its flowers are blue to purple, arranged in a dense spike, with spurred petals and a complex structure that attracts pollinators like bees and butterflies; they bloom in late summer to early autumn. The plant produces follicles as its fruit, containing small, flat seeds that are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, adapted for storing nutrients and surviving harsh conditions.
