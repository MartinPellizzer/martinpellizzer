Aechmea billbergioides

Aechmea billbergioides, commonly known as the silver pine bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its tall, spire-like rosette of narrow, silver-gray leaves that give it its common name. The plant produces a showy inflorescence with bright orange flowers that bloom in the summer months. Its foliage is often used in ornamental horticulture due to its aesthetic appeal and air-purifying qualities.

Aechmea billbergioides thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea billbergioides has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, fleshy, and sword-like, arranged in a compact circle around the central stem.

The leaves are typically dark green with a metallic sheen and can grow up to 30 cm in length. The plant has a short, thick, and somewhat woody stem with a rough, fibrous texture, growing upward from the base of the rosette. Its inflorescence consists of a tall, spadix-like flower spike with small, clustered white to pink flowers that bloom during the rainy season, and are pollinated primarily by insects.

The fruit is a capsule that contains numerous tiny seeds, which are dispersed by wind, while the roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the air and soil.
