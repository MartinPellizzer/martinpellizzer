Aegiphila ternifolia

Aegiphila ternifolia, commonly known as the three-leaved aegiphila, is a flowering plant native to the mountainous regions of South America.

It belongs to the Lamiaceae family, which includes many aromatic herbs and shrubs. The plant is characterized by its small, opposite leaves and clusters of tubular, violet-blue flowers. It thrives in well-drained soils and is often found in rocky or forested habitats.

Due to its ornamental value and medicinal properties, it is sometimes cultivated in gardens and traditional herbal practices.

Morphology

Aegiphila ternifolia has leaves that are trifoliate, meaning they are divided into three leaflets, which are ovate in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, and typically have a dark green color with a slightly hairy surface.

The stem is slender, erect, and branched, with a smooth, light gray bark that becomes rougher with age. Its small, clustered flowers are pinkish-white in color, with five petals and a tubular structure, blooming during the spring and early summer, and are pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, smooth seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapted to anchor the plant in rocky or sandy soils.
