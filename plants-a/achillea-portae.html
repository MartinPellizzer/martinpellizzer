Achillea portae

Achillea portae, commonly known as the Alpine yarrow, is a flowering plant native to the mountainous regions of Central and Southern Europe.

It belongs to the Asteraceae family, characterized by its composite flower heads that resemble small daisies. The plant features silver-gray, hairy leaves and produces clusters of white to pale yellow flowers. It thrives in well-drained soils and is often found in alpine meadows and rocky habitats.

Achillea portae is valued for its ornamental appeal and has been used in traditional herbal medicine.

Morphology

Achillea portae has leaves that are lanceolate in shape, alternate in arrangement, and typically range from 5 to 10 centimeters in length, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is upright, herbaceous, and has a smooth, slightly hairy texture, with a reddish-brown color that becomes more woody with age. The flowers are compound, forming flat-topped clusters called cymes, with white to pale yellow petals and yellow centers, blooming from late spring to early summer, and are primarily pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small achene enclosed in a pappus, which allows for wind dispersal of the seeds.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, helping the plant to spread and establish in various soil types.
