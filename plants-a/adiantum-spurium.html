Adiantum spurium

Adiantum spurium, commonly known as the southern maidenhair fern, is a species of fern native to the southeastern United States.

It is characterized by its delicate, finely divided fronds that resemble the feathers of a bird, hence its common name. The plant prefers moist, shady environments and thrives in rich, well-drained soil. Its leaves are typically bright green and have a glossy appearance.

Adiantum spurium is often used in ornamental gardening due to its elegant and refined foliage.

Morphology

Adiantum spurium has fern-like leaves that are finely divided, with a delicate, lace-like appearance, typically arranged in a bipinnate pattern along the stem.

The leaves are usually small, measuring up to 30 cm in length, and are a bright green color, often turning a rusty brown at the edges when dry. The stem is slender and upright, with a smooth, light green bark that grows in a single, unbranched pattern. Adiantum spurium produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are clustered in spore-bearing structures called sori, which are located on the underside of the leaves, and it relies on wind pollination.

The plant does not produce true flowers or fruits; instead, it disperses its spores through the wind, and its roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring in moist, shaded environments.
