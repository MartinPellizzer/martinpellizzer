Adenophora radiatifolia

Adenophora radiatifolia, commonly known as the radiate leaf bellflower, is a herbaceous perennial native to East Asia.

It features striking, radiating leaves that give it its common name, and it produces clusters of blue to purple flowers. The plant typically grows in moist, well-drained soils in woodland or meadow habitats. Its foliage is characterized by broad, lance-shaped leaves with a velvety texture.

Adenophora radiatifolia is valued in horticulture for its ornamental appeal and ability to thrive in partial shade.

Morphology

Adenophora radiatifolia has leaves that are ovate to lanceolate in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, with a moderate size ranging from 5 to 10 cm in length, and a deep green color.

The stem is slender, slightly hairy, and exhibits a reddish-brown color, growing upright with a branching pattern. Its flowers are bell-shaped, clustered in umbels, with white to pale blue petals, blooming in late spring to early summer, and are primarily pollinated by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and taproot-like, anchoring the plant firmly in the soil.

Native Distribution

Adenophora radiatifolia is primarily native to eastern Asia, including regions such as China, Korea, and Japan.

It is commonly found in moist, shaded forest environments, often along stream banks and in mountainous areas. This species thrives in temperate climates with well-drained soil and partial to full shade. Its native range extends across various provinces in China, particularly in the northeastern and southwestern regions.
