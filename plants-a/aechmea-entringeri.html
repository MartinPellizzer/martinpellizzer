Aechmea entringeri

Aechmea entringeri, commonly known as the Silver Vase Bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients from the environment. The leaves are typically silver or gray in color, with a waxy texture that helps reduce water loss. It produces striking inflorescences with vibrant red or orange flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette.

This plant is popular in horticulture for its ornamental value and ability to thrive in shaded, humid environments.

Morphology

Aechmea entringeri has rosette-shaped leaves that are narrow, pointed, and arranged in a spiral pattern around the plant, with a waxy, dark green color and a sharp, grass-like texture.

The plant lacks a distinct stem, with the leaves directly supporting the inflorescence, and the base of the leaves forms a dense, compact rosette. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix-like structure, with white to pale pink bracts, and they bloom during the dry season, primarily pollinated by insects such as bees and flies. The fruit is a fleshy, berry-like structure that contains small, black seeds, which are dispersed by animals that eat the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in the soil while absorbing moisture and nutrients.
