Aechmea curranii

Aechmea curranii, commonly known as the Curran's bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of South America.

It features a rosette of broad, stiff, and often colorful leaves that form a central cup to collect rainwater. The plant's inflorescence is a tall, branched structure that bears showy, violet to pink flowers. Its foliage is typically green with red or purple markings, adding to its ornamental value.

Aechmea curranii thrives in humid, shady environments and requires well-draining soil and regular misting for optimal growth.

Morphology

Aechmea curranii has leaves that are broad, strap-shaped, and arranged in a rosette, with a waxy, dark green color and a pointed tip.

The plant lacks a true stem, instead forming a dense, compact rosette with a basal cluster of leaves that enclose the central inflorescence. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, typically in shades of yellow to orange, and bloom during the dry season, relying on wind pollination. The fruit is a fleshy, berry-like structure that contains numerous small seeds, which are dispersed by birds and other animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted to absorb moisture from the surrounding soil, supporting the plant's epiphytic growth habit.
