Aechmea augusta

Aechmea augusta, commonly known as the silver vane bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients from the environment. The leaves are typically covered in a dense layer of white or silver hairs, giving the plant its distinctive appearance. It produces vibrant orange or red flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette, adding to its ornamental value.

This plant thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea augusta has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, stiff, and slightly recurved, arranged in a compact spiral at the base, with a dark green to blue-gray coloration.

The plant lacks a true stem, with the leaves forming a dense rosette that encloses a central rosette of leaves and a water-storing central cavity. Its flowers are borne in a spike-like inflorescence, featuring showy, tubular bracts in shades of red, orange, or yellow, blooming during the dry season and pollinated primarily by hummingbirds. The fruit is a fleshy, red berry that contains small seeds, dispersed by birds and mammals that eat the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in its epiphytic or terrestrial habitat.
