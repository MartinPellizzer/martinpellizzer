Aechmea involucrata

Aechmea involucrata, commonly known as the red-tipped pineapple plant, is a striking bromeliad native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients. The plant produces vibrant, spiky flower spikes that range in color from red to orange, often resembling a pinecone. Its leaves are typically dark green with a waxy coating, and the plant thrives in bright, indirect light.

Aechmea involucrata is popular as a decorative plant due to its unique appearance and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea involucrata has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, stiff, and covered with a dense layer of fine, silvery hair, giving them a distinctive grayish-green color.

The leaves are arranged in a tight spiral around the central rosette and can grow up to 30 cm in length. The plant has a short, thick, underground stem with a fibrous, rough-textured bark. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, and the flowers are typically pink or red, blooming during the summer months.

The plant is pollinated by insects, and it produces a fleshy, red berry containing numerous seeds that are dispersed by birds and animals.
