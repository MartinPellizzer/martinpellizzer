Aconitum obtusifolium

Aconitum obtusifolium, commonly known as the blunt-leaved monkshood, is a herbaceous perennial native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It features striking purple to blue flowers arranged in a helmet-like structure, typical of the genus Aconitum. The plant has broad, heart-shaped leaves with a distinctive waxy texture and deep green color. It thrives in cool, moist environments and is often found in woodland or alpine habitats.

However, all parts of the plant are highly toxic due to the presence of potent alkaloids.

Its flowers are blue to purple, arranged in dense, sparsely branched racemes, with showy spurred petals and a short blooming period in late summer; they are pollinated by bees and other insects. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and fibrous, often forming a dense, tuberous mass that helps the plant survive in harsh conditions.

Native Distribution

Aconitum obtusifolium is primarily native to the mountainous regions of central and eastern Europe, including countries such as Austria, Switzerland, Germany, and the Czech Republic.

It thrives in alpine and subalpine environments, often growing on rocky slopes and in meadows at elevations ranging from 1,000 to 2,500 meters. This species is particularly common in the Carpathian and Alps mountain ranges, where it benefits from the cool, moist conditions. It is also found in parts of northern Italy and southern Poland.

Due to its preference for high-altitude habitats, it is relatively rare in lower elevations and is considered a characteristic plant of the European mountain flora.

Introduced Distribution
