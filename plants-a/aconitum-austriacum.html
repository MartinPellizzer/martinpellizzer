Aconitum austriacum

Aconitum austriacum, commonly known as the Austrian monkshood, is a herbaceous perennial plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Europe.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, hood-shaped blue to purple flowers. The plant features large, palmately lobed leaves that are deeply divided and covered in a dense layer of fine hairs. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade, often found in woodland or alpine habitats.

Due to its toxic nature, it should be handled with care, as all parts of the plant contain potent alkaloids.

Morphology

Aconitum austriacum has palmately compound leaves that are deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside, arranged alternately along the stem.

The stem is smooth and cylindrical, with a dark brown to black bark that becomes rougher with age, growing upright and branching towards the top. The flowers are blue to purple, bell-shaped, and arranged in dense spikes, blooming in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated primarily by bees. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, flat, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are deep and fibrous, helping the plant to thrive in moist, well-drained soils.
