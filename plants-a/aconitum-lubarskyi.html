Aconitum lubarskyi

Aconitum lubarskyi, commonly known as the Lubarsky aconite, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stems that can reach heights of up to 2 meters. The plant produces striking blue to purple flowers arranged in dense clusters at the top of the stem. Its leaves are deeply lobed and have a rough, hairy texture.

Aconitum lubarskyi contains potent alkaloids, making it highly toxic and requiring careful handling.

Morphology

Aconitum lubarskyi has large, compound leaves that are palmately arranged, with deeply lobed blades that are typically green on top and slightly hairy underneath.

The stem is erect and cylindrical, with a smooth, dark brown bark that has a slightly rough texture. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, and consist of five petals arranged in a spurred shape, blooming in late summer and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a follicle that contains numerous small, black seeds dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, which help the plant store nutrients and survive in its native habitat.
