Aechmea suaveolens

Aechmea suaveolens, commonly known as the sweet-scented bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients from the environment. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences that range in color from red to orange, often resembling a torch. Its foliage is typically a deep green with a waxy texture, and it thrives in bright, indirect light.

Aechmea suaveolens is popular as a decorative plant due to its unique appearance and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea suaveolens has rosette-shaped leaves that are fleshy, strap-like, and often have a waxy texture, arranged in a compact circle around the central stem.

The leaves are typically blue-green in color, with some varieties displaying a striking purple or red hue along the edges. The plant has a short, thick stem that is often covered in a layer of moss-like material, and it grows upright with a rosette formation. Its flowers are borne in a spike at the center of the rosette, with white or pale blue petals and a distinctive red or purple central column, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by insects.

The fruit is a small, capsule-like structure that disperses seeds through wind and animal activity, and the roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the air.
