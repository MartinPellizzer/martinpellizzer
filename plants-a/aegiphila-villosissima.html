Aegiphila villosissima

Aegiphila villosissima, commonly known as the hairy aegiphila, is a shrub native to the Caribbean, particularly found in the Bahamas and Cuba.

It is characterized by its dense, velvety foliage that gives it a distinctive, soft appearance. The plant produces small, white flowers that bloom in clusters, adding ornamental value to its overall appearance. It thrives in well-drained soils and full sun, making it suitable for coastal and sandy environments.

Due to its unique appearance and ecological role, it is often cultivated in botanical gardens and conservation areas.

Morphology

Aegiphila villosissima has leaves that are ovate to lanceolate in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, with a hairy surface and a dark green color.

The stem is hairy and somewhat woody, with a reddish-brown bark that becomes smoother as the plant matures. Its small, white flowers are clustered in panicles, blooming in the summer months and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and adapted to anchor the plant in rocky or arid soils.
