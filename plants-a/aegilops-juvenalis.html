Aegilops juvenalis

Aegilops juvenalis, commonly known as the wild emmer wheat, is a wild grass species native to the Mediterranean region.

It is closely related to modern wheat and is considered a progenitor of domesticated wheat varieties. The plant has narrow, grass-like leaves and produces small, spikelet-like inflorescences. It is characterized by its hardy nature and ability to thrive in arid conditions.

Its genetic diversity is of significant interest in agricultural research for improving wheat resilience and yield.

Morphology

Aegilops juvenalis has narrow, grass-like leaves that are linear in shape, alternate in arrangement, and typically range from 5 to 15 centimeters in length, with a bluish-green color.

The stem is cylindrical, smooth, and slightly hairy, with a pale green color and grows upright. It produces small, spike-like flowers that are greenish-yellow in color, blooming in early summer, and are pollinated by wind. The fruit is a small, dry capsule that disperses seeds by wind, and the seeds are small and oval in shape.
