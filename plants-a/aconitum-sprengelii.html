Aconitum sprengelii

Aconitum sprengelii, commonly known as Sprengel's monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its striking, helmet-shaped flowers that are typically blue or purple in color. The plant features palmately compound leaves with a distinctive, spurred petal at the back of its flowers. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and is often found in alpine meadows and rocky habitats.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it is considered a potentially dangerous plant to handle.

Morphology

Aconitum sprengelii has compound, palmately arranged leaves that are deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes hairy underside.

The stem is upright, smooth, and somewhat woody, with a grayish-brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, with five petal-like sepals and numerous yellow stamens, blooming in late summer and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds dispersed by wind.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, adapted for storing nutrients and surviving in cold, mountainous environments.
