Achillea rompelii

Achillea rompelii, commonly known as the Roman hemlock, is a flowering plant native to the Mediterranean region.

It belongs to the Asteraceae family and is characterized by its dark green, fern-like leaves and showy yellow flowers. The plant typically grows in rocky or dry soils and thrives in full sun. It is often found in coastal areas and can tolerate saline conditions.

Achillea rompelii is valued for its ornamental appeal and historical use in traditional medicine.

Morphology

Achillea rompelii has leaves that are lanceolate to ovate in shape, with a silver-gray underside and a dark green upper surface, arranged alternately along the stem.

The stems are upright, hairy, and have a reddish-brown color, growing in a clumping habit. Its flowers are composite, consisting of yellow disk flowers surrounded by white ray flowers, blooming in late spring to early summer, and are primarily pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small achene, dispersed by wind due to its feathery pappus.
