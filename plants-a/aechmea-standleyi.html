Aechmea standleyi

Aechmea standleyi, commonly known as the Christmas bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the humid forests of Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped foliage, which forms a central cup to collect rainwater, providing a habitat for small organisms. The plant's leaves are typically dark green with a waxy texture, and they often display intricate patterns or stripes. It produces vibrant inflorescences with brightly colored bracts that range from red to orange, often resembling a flower.

Aechmea standleyi is well-suited for indoor cultivation due to its adaptability and ornamental value.

Morphology

Aechmea standleyi has rosette-shaped leaves that are narrow, sword-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a bright green color and a waxy texture.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, thick base that supports the rosette of leaves, which are often covered in a dense layer of fine hairs. Its inflorescence consists of a tall, branched spike bearing small, clustered flowers that are typically pink or red, blooming during the rainy season and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a capsule that releases tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and adapted to absorb moisture from the humid environment where the plant grows.
