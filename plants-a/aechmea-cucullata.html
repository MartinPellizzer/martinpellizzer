Aechmea cucullata

Aechmea cucullata, commonly known as the hooded bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients. The plant's foliage is typically dark green with a waxy texture, and it produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences in shades of red, orange, or yellow. Aechmea cucullata thrives in humid, shaded environments and is often found growing on trees or rocks.

Its unique structure and ornamental value make it a popular choice for indoor and outdoor gardening.

Morphology

Aechmea cucullata has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, sword-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a waxy, dark green color and a slightly curled tip.

The plant lacks a typical stem, with the base forming a dense, fibrous rosette that serves as a water reservoir. Its inflorescence consists of a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, with small white to pink flowers that bloom during the summer months and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a fleshy, red berry that contains numerous seeds, which are dispersed by birds and mammals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the air and soil, typical of epiphytic growth.
