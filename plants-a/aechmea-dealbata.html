Aechmea dealbata

Aechmea dealbata, commonly known as the White-tipped Bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the cloud forests of Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaf structure, which forms a central cup to collect rainwater. The leaves are typically dark green with a white or silver band near the tips, giving it its common name. It produces showy, long-lasting inflorescences with vibrant red or orange flowers.

This plant thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular as a decorative houseplant due to its ornamental appearance.

Morphology

Aechmea dealbata has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, fleshy, and often covered in a dense layer of white hairs, giving them a silvery appearance.

The leaves are arranged in a tight spiral around the central rosette, with a waxy, bluish-green color and can grow up to 30 cm in length. The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, compact growth pattern with a thick, fibrous trunk that resembles a pseudobark. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, typically in shades of red or pink, and bloom during the summer months, relying on wind pollination.

The fruit is a fleshy, berry-like structure that contains numerous small seeds, which are dispersed by birds and other animals.
