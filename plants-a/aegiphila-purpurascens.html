Aegiphila purpurascens

Aegiphila purpurascens, commonly known as the purpleleaf shrub, is a perennial plant native to the dry forests of South America.

It belongs to the Lamiaceae family, which includes many aromatic herbs and shrubs. The plant is characterized by its aromatic, fuzzy leaves that turn a striking purple color as they mature. It produces small, pale blue flowers that bloom in the spring, attracting various pollinators.

Aegiphila purpurascens is often used in traditional medicine for its purported anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties.

Morphology

Aegiphila purpurascens has compound leaves that are opposite in arrangement, with lanceolate to ovate leaflets, typically ranging from 2 to 5 cm in length, and displaying a dark green to reddish-purple coloration.

The stem is upright and cylindrical, with a smooth, reddish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age, and it exhibits a branching growth pattern. The flowers are small, clustered in terminal inflorescences, with tubular corollas and violet to purple petals, blooming during the dry season, and are pollinated by insects such as bees. The fruit is a dry, dehiscent capsule containing numerous tiny seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and extensive, aiding in water absorption from the dry soil.
