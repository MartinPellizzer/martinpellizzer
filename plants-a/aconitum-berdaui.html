Aconitum berdaui

Aconitum berdaui, commonly known as the Balkan aconite, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of the Balkans.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stems that can reach up to 2 meters in height. The plant produces showy, blue to purple flowers in late summer, arranged in dense, spikelike inflorescences. Its leaves are palmately compound, with deeply cut lobes, and its roots contain toxic alkaloids.

Due to its toxicity, it is considered a hazardous plant and is often avoided in horticulture.

Morphology

Aconitum berdaui has compound, palmately lobed leaves that are typically dark green on top and lighter underneath, with a rough texture and a basal arrangement.

The stem is upright, hairy, and has a reddish-brown bark that becomes smoother as the plant matures. Its flowers are blue to purple, bell-shaped, and arranged in dense clusters, blooming in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, flat seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, adapted for storage and anchoring the plant in moist, shady environments.
