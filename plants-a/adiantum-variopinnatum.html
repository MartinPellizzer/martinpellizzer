Adiantum variopinnatum

Adiantum variopinnatum, commonly known as the variable maidenhair fern, is a delicate and elegant fern native to the southeastern United States.

It is characterized by its finely divided, lace-like fronds that are typically dark green with a silvery underside. The plant prefers shady, moist environments and thrives in well-drained, acidic soils. Its fronds are biennial, meaning they live for two years before dying and being replaced by new growth.

Adiantum variopinnatum is valued for its ornamental appeal and is often cultivated in gardens and as a houseplant.

Morphology

Adiantum variopinnatum has fern-like leaves that are finely divided, pinnately arranged, and typically have a delicate, lacey appearance.

The leaves are usually light green, with a slender, upright growth pattern and can reach up to 30 cm in length. The plant has a short, unbranched stem with a smooth, pale green bark. It produces small, inconspicuous flowers that are clustered in spore-bearing structures called sori, which are located on the underside of the fronds, and it relies on wind pollination.

The fruit is not present, as it reproduces via spores, and the roots are fibrous, helping to anchor the plant and absorb moisture from the soil.
