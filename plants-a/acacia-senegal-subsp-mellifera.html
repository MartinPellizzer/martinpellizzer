Acacia senegal subsp. mellifera

Acacia senegal subsp.

mellifera, commonly known as the gum arabic acacia, is a shrub or small tree native to the Sahel region of Africa. It is characterized by its slender, often multi-branched stems and bipinnate leaves that are covered in fine, silky hairs.

The plant produces yellow flowers that are clustered in cylindrical inflorescences, followed by elongated, woody pods containing a sticky, resinous substance called gum arabic. This resin is harvested for use in food, pharmaceuticals, and industrial applications.

The plant thrives in arid and semi-arid environments, making it an important species in desert ecosystems.

Native Distribution

Acacia senegal subsp.

mellifera is primarily native to the Sahel region of Africa, including countries such as Sudan, Niger, Chad, and Mali. It thrives in semi-arid and arid environments, often found in savannas and dry woodlands.

This subspecies is well adapted to hot, dry climates with seasonal rainfall. It plays an important ecological role in stabilizing soil and providing habitat for various wildlife.

Additionally, it is used locally for its gum arabic production and traditional medicinal purposes.
