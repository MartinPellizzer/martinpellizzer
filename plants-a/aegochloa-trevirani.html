Aegochloa trevirani

Aegochloa trevirani, commonly known as the Teweran grass, is a perennial grass native to the wetlands of southern Africa.

It is characterized by its tall, slender stems and narrow, blade-like leaves that are often covered in fine hairs. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences that contain numerous tiny flowers, typical of the grass family. It thrives in moist, clayey soils and is often found in marshy areas or along riverbanks.

Due to its resilience and ability to stabilize soil, it plays an important ecological role in its native habitat.

Morphology

Aegochloa trevirani has narrow, grass-like leaves that are linear in shape, alternate in arrangement, and typically range from 10 to 30 centimeters in length, with a pale green to bluish-gray color.

The stem is cylindrical, with a smooth texture and a pale yellowish-brown color, growing in dense clumps or tufts. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences with tiny, pale green to white flowers that bloom during the summer months, and are wind-pollinated. The fruit is a small, dry, three-angled achene, dispersed by wind due to its lightweight structure.
