Aechmea lateralis

Aechmea lateralis, commonly known as the silver pine bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of Central and South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaf structure, which forms a central cup to collect water and nutrients. The leaves are typically long, narrow, and covered in a silvery-gray coating, giving it its common name. It produces striking inflorescences with vibrant red or orange flowers that bloom in the summer months.

This plant is well adapted to humid, shaded environments and is often cultivated as an ornamental species in gardens and greenhouses.

Morphology

Aechmea lateralis has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, fleshy, and dark green with a waxy coating, arranged in a tight spiral around the central rosette.

The plant has a short, thick stem that is often hidden beneath the leaves, with a smooth, pale green bark that remains compact and unbranched. Its inflorescence consists of a dense spike of violet to purple flowers, which bloom in the late spring to early summer, and are pollinated by insects such as bees and butterflies. The fruit is a red, fleshy berry that contains small, black seeds, which are dispersed by birds and water.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for absorbing moisture from the surrounding soil.
