Aechmea fraseri

Aechmea fraseri, commonly known as the Fraser's bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the subtropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect water. The plant's foliage is typically vibrant green with a bluish or purple tinge, and it produces striking inflorescences with colorful bracts. It thrives in humid, shaded environments and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Aechmea fraseri is popular as a decorative houseplant due to its aesthetic appeal and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea fraseri has rosette-shaped leaves that are stiff, sword-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a dark green color and a waxy surface.

The plant has a short, unbranched stem that is covered in a thick, fibrous sheath, and it grows upright with a compact form. Its inflorescence consists of a spike of pale pink to white flowers, which bloom in late summer to early autumn and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a fleshy, red berry that contains small, black seeds, which are dispersed by animals eating the fruit.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in the soil, with some rhizomes extending from the base.
