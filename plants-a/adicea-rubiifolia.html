Adicea rubiifolia

Adicea rubiifolia, commonly known as the red-leafed dogwood, is a deciduous shrub native to the eastern United States.

It features striking, velvety red foliage that turns vibrant orange in the fall. The plant produces clusters of small, white flowers in the spring, which are followed by bright red berries. Adicea rubiifolia thrives in partial to full shade and moist, well-drained soil.

Its ornamental value makes it a popular choice for woodland gardens and naturalized landscapes.

The stems are slender, upright, and have a smooth, reddish-brown bark that becomes more fissured with age. Its small, clustered flowers are white to pale pink, with five petals and numerous stamens, blooming in late spring to early summer and are primarily pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small, dry capsule that splits open to release seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in moist, acidic soils.

Native Distribution

Adicea rubiifolia is primarily native to eastern and southern Africa, including countries such as Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, and South Africa.

It is commonly found in grasslands, woodlands, and along riverbanks in these regions. The plant thrives in areas with well-drained soils and moderate to high rainfall. It is often associated with disturbed habitats and can be found in both natural and cultivated settings.

Adicea rubiifolia is an important component of the local flora and is sometimes used in traditional medicine.
