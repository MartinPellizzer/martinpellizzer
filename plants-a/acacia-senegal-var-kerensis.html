Acacia senegal var. kerensis

Acacia senegal var. kerensis, commonly known as the gum arabic tree, is a deciduous shrub or small tree native to the Sahel region of Africa.

It is characterized by its slender, branching structure and phyllodes that are narrow and often curved. The plant produces yellow flowers and is notable for its ability to fix nitrogen, contributing to soil fertility. Its most valuable feature is the production of gum arabic, a sap used in food, pharmaceuticals, and industrial applications.

This variety is adapted to arid and semi-arid environments, making it an important resource in dryland ecosystems.

Native Distribution

Acacia senegal var. kerensis is primarily native to the Sahel region of Africa, including countries such as Sudan, South Sudan, Eritrea, Ethiopia, and parts of Somalia.

It thrives in semi-arid and arid environments, often found in sandy soils and along riverbanks. This variant is well adapted to the harsh climatic conditions of the region, including high temperatures and limited rainfall. It plays an important ecological role in stabilizing soils and providing habitat for various wildlife species.

Additionally, it is used locally for its gum arabic production and as a source of timber and fuel.

Introduced Distribution

Acacia senegal var. kerensis was mainly introduced to regions of the Sahel and parts of East Africa for its gum arabic production and ecological benefits.

It is commonly found in semi-arid and dry savanna areas where it contributes to soil stabilization and provides habitat for local wildlife.
