Adyseton obtusifolium

Adyseton obtusifolium, commonly known as the broadleaf adyseton, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the eastern United States.

It typically grows in moist, woodland habitats and features lance-shaped leaves with a smooth, waxy surface. The plant produces small, clustered flowers that are usually white or pale green, blooming in late spring to early summer. Its root system is fibrous and often used in traditional herbal medicine.

Adyseton obtusifolium is characterized by its slow growth and preference for shaded environments.

Morphology

Adyseton obtusifolium has leaves that are broad and ovate, with a rounded tip, arranged alternately along the stem, and typically ranging from 5 to 10 cm in length, with a dull green color.

The stem is erect and somewhat woody, with a rough, fissured bark that is brown in color. The flowers are small, clustered in panicles, with white petals and yellow stamens, blooming in early spring, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in moist, well-drained soils.
