Aegiphila velutina

Aegiphila velutina, commonly known as velvetleaf aegiphila, is a shrub native to the Caribbean and parts of Central America.

It is characterized by its hairy, velvety leaves that give it its common name. The plant typically grows in moist, shaded environments, often in tropical and subtropical regions. Its small, clustered flowers are usually white or pale yellow, and it produces small, round fruits.

Aegiphila velutina is valued for its ornamental appeal and is sometimes used in traditional medicine.

Morphology

Aegiphila velutina has leaves that are lanceolate, arranged alternately along the stem, with a velvety texture and a dark green color.

The stem is erect, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that becomes slightly rough with age. Its flowers are small, clustered in umbels, with white petals and yellow stamens, blooming in early spring and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapting to rocky soils in its native habitat.
