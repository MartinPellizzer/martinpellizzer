Aechmea iguana

Aechmea iguana, commonly known as the iguana bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaf structure that forms a central cup to collect water. The leaves are typically long, narrow, and covered in a dense layer of trichomes, which help in water retention and protection from intense sunlight. The plant produces vibrant, showy inflorescences with colorful bracts that attract pollinators.

It thrives in humid, shaded environments and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea iguana has rosette-shaped leaves that are stiff, leathery, and arranged in a spiral around the central rosette, with a bluish-green color and a waxy texture.

The plant has a short, thick stem that is often hidden beneath the leaves, with a smooth, pale green bark that grows upright. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix, surrounded by a colorful bract, and are typically pink or red, blooming during the dry season; they are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small, fleshy capsule that contains numerous seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in its habitat, often growing in rocky or well-drained soil.
