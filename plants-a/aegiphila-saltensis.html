Aegiphila saltensis

Aegiphila saltensis, commonly known as the saltbush, is a shrub native to arid and semi-arid regions of South America.

It is characterized by its small, lance-shaped leaves that are often covered in a waxy cuticle to reduce water loss. The plant produces clusters of small, white or pale yellow flowers during the spring and summer months. It is adapted to saline and alkaline soils, making it a valuable plant for soil stabilization in harsh environments.

Aegiphila saltensis is also used in traditional medicine for its purported health benefits.

Morphology

Aegiphila saltensis has leaves that are ovate to elliptical in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, with a medium size and a silvery-green color due to a dense covering of fine hairs.

The stem is cylindrical, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age, and it grows upright with a branching pattern. The flowers are small, clustered in inflorescences, with white petals and yellow stamens, blooming during the dry season, and are pollinated by wind. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release small, winged seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are shallow and fibrous, adapted to absorb water efficiently from the arid environment.
