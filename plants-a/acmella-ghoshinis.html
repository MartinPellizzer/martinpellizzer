Acmella ghoshinis

Acmella ghoshinis, commonly known as the ghost flower, is a member of the Acanthaceae family and is native to tropical regions of Asia.

It is characterized by its small, bright yellow or orange flowers that resemble tiny suns, which bloom in clusters. The plant typically grows as an annual herb with erect, hairy stems and lance-shaped leaves. It thrives in warm, humid conditions and prefers well-drained soil.

Its ornamental value makes it a popular choice for gardens and floral arrangements.

Morphology

Acmella ghoshinis has leaves that are ovate to elliptical in shape, arranged alternately along the stem, with a moderate size ranging from 2 to 5 cm in length, and a glossy green color.

The stem is slender, slightly hairy, and has a light green to pale brown color, growing upright with a slightly branched pattern. Its flowers are small, zygomorphic, with a yellow center and red or orange petals, blooming in the summer months, and are primarily pollinated by bees. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind and water.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, helping the plant anchor in moist, well-drained soils.
