Aconitum suspensum

Aconitum suspensum, commonly known as the hanging monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It features striking blue to purple flowers arranged in a spire-like inflorescence, typical of the Ranunculaceae family. The plant has palmately lobed leaves that are deeply divided, giving it a distinctive appearance. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Due to its toxic alkaloids, it is important to handle the plant with care.

Morphology

Aconitum suspensum has leaves that are compound, typically with three to five leaflets, arranged alternately along the stem, and are usually dark green with a velvety texture.

The stem is erect, smooth, and often slightly hairy, with a grayish-brown bark that becomes woody as the plant matures. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, with five petals and a long, narrow spurs, blooming in late summer to early autumn and pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a follicle that releases small, winged seeds through wind dispersal.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, anchoring the plant and storing nutrients.
