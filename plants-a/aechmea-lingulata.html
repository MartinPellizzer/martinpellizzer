Aechmea lingulata

Aechmea lingulata, commonly known as the velvet bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical rainforests of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects rainwater and provides a habitat for small organisms. The leaves are typically dark green with a velvety texture and often have a bluish or purplish hue. It produces vibrant orange or red flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette, adding dramatic visual contrast.

This plant thrives in humid, shaded environments and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea lingulata has rosette-shaped leaves that are strap-like, stiff, and dark green with a waxy texture, arranged in a compact circle around the central stem.

The stem is short and thick, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that grows upward from the base of the plant. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix surrounded by colorful bracts, typically pink or red, blooming in late spring to early summer, and are pollinated by insects such as bees and flies. The fruit is a capsule that contains numerous small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind and water.

The roots are fibrous and adventitious, anchoring the plant and absorbing moisture from the surrounding environment.
