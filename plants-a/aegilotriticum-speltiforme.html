Aegilotriticum speltiforme

Aegilotriticum speltiforme, commonly known as false spelt, is a grass species native to central and eastern Europe.

It is characterized by its tall, slender stems and narrow, parallel-veined leaves. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences that contain numerous tiny flowers. It thrives in moist, disturbed soils and is often found in meadows and along riverbanks.

Due to its historical use in agriculture, it has been studied for its potential as a resilient cereal grain.

Morphology

Aegilotriticum speltiforme has leaves that are narrow and linear, arranged in a spiral pattern along the stem, with a bluish-green color and a smooth surface.

The stem is cylindrical, covered in a rough, fibrous bark that is light brown in color, and grows upright with a branching pattern. Its small, clustered flowers are yellowish-green in color, with a simple structure consisting of six petals, blooming in late spring and pollinated by wind. The fruit is a capsule that releases tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and spread out just beneath the soil surface, anchoring the plant and absorbing nutrients.
