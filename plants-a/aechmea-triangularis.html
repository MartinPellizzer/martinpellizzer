Aechmea triangularis

Aechmea triangularis, commonly known as the triangular bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical rainforests of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients from the environment. The leaves are typically dark green with a waxy surface and often exhibit a distinctive triangular shape, giving the plant its common name. It produces vibrant orange or red flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette, adding a dramatic visual contrast.

This plant thrives in humid, indirect light conditions and is popular as a decorative houseplant due to its unique appearance and low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea triangularis has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, stiff, and covered in fine hairs, arranged in a tight spiral around the plant's central axis.

The leaves are typically dark green with a silvery underside and can grow up to 30 cm in length. The plant has a short, thick, and somewhat woody stem that is usually greenish in color, growing from the base of the leaf rosette. Its inflorescence consists of a tall, spiky flower stem with clustered violet to purple flowers that bloom during the summer months, and they are pollinated by insects such as bees and beetles.

The plant produces a small, berry-like fruit that contains numerous seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.
