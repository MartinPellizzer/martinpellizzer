Aegiphila hirsutissima

Aegiphila hirsutissima, commonly known as the hairy aegiphila, is a perennial shrub native to the Andean regions of South America.

It is characterized by its dense, hairy foliage and small, clustered flowers that range in color from white to pink. The plant typically grows in high-altitude environments, often in rocky or gravelly soils. Its leaves are aromatic and have been used in traditional medicine for their purported health benefits.

Aegiphila hirsutissima is valued for its ornamental appeal and ecological role in its native habitat.

Morphology

Aegiphila hirsutissima has leaves that are ovate to elliptic in shape, with a hairy surface and a dark green color, arranged alternately along the stem.

The stem is erect and branching, with a rough, grayish-brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its small, clustered flowers are pinkish-white in color, with five petals and a tubular corolla, blooming from April to June and pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapting to the dry, rocky soils of its native habitat.
