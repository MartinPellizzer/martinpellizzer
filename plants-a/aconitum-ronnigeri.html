Aconitum ronnigeri

Aconitum ronnigeri, commonly known as the Ronniger's aconite, is a member of the Ranunculaceae family and is native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It is a herbaceous perennial that produces striking blue to purple flowers during the summer months. The plant features a stout, upright stem with palmately divided leaves that are deeply lobed. Its roots contain toxic alkaloids, making it a potentially dangerous plant to handle.

It thrives in well-drained soils and prefers partial to full sunlight.

Morphology

Aconitum ronnigeri has compound, palmately arranged leaves that are deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter, sometimes hairy underside.

The stem is upright, smooth, and slightly hairy, with a greenish-brown bark that becomes darker with age. Its flowers are blue to violet, arranged in dense spikes, featuring showy petals and sepals, and bloom in late summer; they are primarily pollinated by bees. The fruit is a follicle that releases seeds through a thin membrane, and the seeds are dispersed by wind.

The roots are tuberous and fibrous, adapted for storing nutrients and surviving in cooler, moist environments.
