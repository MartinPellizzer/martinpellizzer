Aconitum clairvilleanum

Aconitum clairvilleanum, commonly known as the Clairville aconite, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stem and showy, blue to purple flowers. The plant produces smooth, ovate leaves with a velvety texture and is known for its toxic alkaloids, which can be harmful to humans and animals. Aconitum clairvilleanum typically grows in rocky or well-drained soils at high altitudes.

It is often cultivated in gardens for its ornamental value, though care must be taken due to its potent toxicity.

Morphology

Aconitum clairvilleanum has compound, palmately lobed leaves that are typically bright green, with a slightly hairy texture, and arranged alternately along the stem.

The stem is upright, cylindrical, and covered with a smooth, grayish-brown bark, growing to a height of up to 1.5 meters. Its flowers are showy, blue to purple in color, with five petal-like sepals and numerous stamens, blooming in late summer and attracting pollinators such as bees and butterflies through nectar. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, tuberous, and adapted for storing nutrients, allowing the plant to survive in cooler, moist environments.
