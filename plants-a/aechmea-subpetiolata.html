Aechmea subpetiolata

Aechmea subpetiolata, commonly known as the glassy bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to South America.

It features a rosette-shaped inflorescence with vibrant, long-lasting flowers that range from yellow to orange. The plant's leaves are thick, fleshy, and covered in a waxy cuticle, which helps it retain moisture in its natural habitat. It thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Aechmea subpetiolata is popular as a decorative plant due to its unique appearance and relatively easy care requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea subpetiolata has rosette-shaped leaves that are narrow, grass-like, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a dark green color and a waxy texture.

The plant lacks a distinct stem, but its basal rosette is surrounded by a dense, compact structure that resembles a pseudostem. It produces inflorescences with vibrant red and orange flowers that bloom during the dry season, and they are pollinated primarily by birds. The fruit is a small, elongated berry that disperses its seeds through wind and animal consumption.

Its roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring in the soil, with some rhizomes contributing to the plant's clonal growth.
