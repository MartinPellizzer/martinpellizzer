Aegiphila arcta

Aegiphila arcta, commonly known as the narrow-leafed aegiphila, is a perennial shrub native to the arid regions of South America.

It belongs to the Lamiaceae family, which includes many aromatic plants, and is characterized by its small, lance-shaped leaves. The plant produces clusters of tubular, two-lipped flowers that are typically pink or purple in color. It thrives in dry, sandy soils and is adapted to withstand high temperatures and low precipitation.

Due to its ornamental value and drought tolerance, it is occasionally cultivated in xeriscaped gardens.

Morphology

Aegiphila arcta has small, ovate leaves that are arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green color and a slightly hairy surface.

The stem is slender and upright, with a smooth, reddish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age. Its flowers are clustered in dense spikes, featuring small, tubular structures with purple petals, blooming in spring and attracting bees for pollination. The fruit is a dry capsule that splits open to release tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, helping the plant anchor itself in dry, rocky soils.
