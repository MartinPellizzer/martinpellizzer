Aegiphila falcata

Aegiphila falcata, commonly known as the sickle-leaved aegiphila, is a perennial shrub native to the Caribbean and parts of Central America.

It belongs to the Lamiaceae family, which includes many aromatic herbs and shrubs. The plant is characterized by its narrow, sickle-shaped leaves that are often covered in fine hairs. It produces small, pale purple to white flowers arranged in whorled clusters along the stems.

Aegiphila falcata is adapted to grow in dry, rocky soils and is often found in coastal or semi-arid regions.

Morphology

Aegiphila falcata has leaves that are narrow, lance-shaped, and often have a waxy coating, arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green color and varying in size from 2 to 6 centimeters in length.

The stem is upright, with a rough, bark-like texture and a reddish-brown color, growing in a bushy pattern. Its flowers are small, clustered in dense inflorescences, with pinkish-white petals and yellow stamens, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by insects. The fruit is a small, dry capsule that splits open to release tiny, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapted to absorb water from the dry, sandy soils in which the plant thrives.
