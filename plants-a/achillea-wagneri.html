Achillea wagneri

Achillea wagneri, commonly known as Wagner's yarrow, is a perennial flowering plant native to southern Europe and parts of western Asia.

It belongs to the Asteraceae family, characterized by its composite flower heads that resemble small daisies. The plant features gray-green, fern-like leaves and produces clusters of tiny white to pale yellow flowers. It thrives in well-drained soils and full sun, often found in rocky or dry habitats.

Its ornamental value makes it a popular choice in gardens for its hardiness and attractive appearance.

Morphology

Achillea wagneri has leaves that are lanceolate in shape, alternate in arrangement, and typically range from 5 to 15 cm in length, with a gray-green color due to a dense covering of fine hairs.

The stems are cylindrical, hairy, and have a reddish-brown color, growing upright with a branching pattern. The flowers are composite, consisting of yellow disk flowers surrounded by white ray flowers, blooming from June to August, and are pollinated primarily by insects. The fruit is a cylindrical achene, and the seeds are dispersed by wind due to their lightweight structure.

The roots are fibrous and rhizomatous, allowing the plant to spread vegetatively.
