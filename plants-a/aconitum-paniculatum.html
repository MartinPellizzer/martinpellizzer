Aconitum paniculatum

Aconitum paniculatum, commonly known as the European monkshood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to Europe and parts of Asia.

It is characterized by its striking purple-blue flowers arranged in a tall, conical inflorescence. The plant has dark green, palmately lobed leaves that are often covered in a fine pubescence. It thrives in moist, well-drained soils and prefers partial to full shade.

Aconitum paniculatum contains potent alkaloids, making it toxic and requiring caution during handling.

Morphology

Aconitum paniculatum has leaves that are compound, consisting of several lance-shaped leaflets arranged alternately along the stem, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is tall, upright, and often branched, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that becomes rougher with age. Its flowers are blue to purple, arranged in panicles, with showy spurred petals and a distinctive structure that attracts pollinators like bees and butterflies; they bloom in late summer. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, dark seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, often forming a dense network that helps the plant store nutrients and survive harsh conditions.
