Aegiphila sylvatica

Aegiphila sylvatica, commonly known as the Brazilian pennyroyal, is a perennial shrub native to the Atlantic Forest of Brazil.

It features aromatic, narrow, lance-shaped leaves with a distinctive silvery underside. The plant produces small, clustered white or pale yellow flowers that bloom in late spring to early summer. It thrives in well-drained soils and partial to full sunlight.

Due to its ornamental value and medicinal properties, it is occasionally cultivated in gardens and traditional herbal practices.

Morphology

Aegiphila sylvatica has leaves that are narrow, lance-shaped, and arranged alternately along the stems, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside.

The stem is slender, somewhat woody, with a rough, greyish bark that becomes more fissured with age. Its small, clustered flowers are pinkish-white, with five petals and a tubular corolla, blooming in spring and attracting pollinators like bees through nectar. The fruit is a dry, capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, adapted for efficient water absorption in its native arid or semi-arid habitats.
