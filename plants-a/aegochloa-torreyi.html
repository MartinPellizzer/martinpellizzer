Aegochloa torreyi

Aegochloa torreyi, commonly known as Torrey's grass, is a perennial grass native to the southwestern United States and northern Mexico.

It is characterized by its tufted growth habit and narrow, grass-like leaves that are often covered in fine hairs. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences that contain numerous tiny, wind-dispersed seeds. It thrives in arid and semi-arid environments, often found in disturbed soils or along roadsides.

Botanically, it is classified within the Poaceae family and is notable for its ecological adaptability and role in stabilizing sandy or rocky soils.

Morphology

Aegochloa torreyi has narrow, grass-like leaves that are typically linear in shape, arranged in a basal rosette, and have a bluish-green color with a slightly hairy texture.

The stem is erect, with a smooth, light green to reddish-brown bark that becomes more woody with age. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences bearing tiny, pale blue to purple flowers that bloom in late spring to early summer, and are primarily pollinated by wind.

The fruit is a small, dry, three-angled capsule that disperses seeds through wind, and the roots are fibrous and well-developed, anchoring the plant in moist soil.
