Aconitum triste

Aconitum triste, commonly known as the melancholy monk's hood, is a perennial herbaceous plant native to the mountainous regions of Central Asia.

It belongs to the Ranunculaceae family and is characterized by its tall, sparsely branched stems and showy, hood-shaped flowers that are typically blue to purple in color. The plant produces distinctive, helmet-like flowers with a spurred petal at the back, which is a defining feature of the genus Aconitum. Its foliage consists of palmately compound leaves with deeply cut lobes, contributing to its ornamental appeal.

However, all parts of the plant are highly toxic due to the presence of potent alkaloids, making it a dangerous species to handle without proper precautions.

Morphology

Aconitum triste has large, compound leaves that are deeply lobed, with a dark green upper surface and a lighter underside, arranged alternately along the stem.

The stem is upright, hairy, and has a greenish-brown bark that becomes rough with age. Its flowers are blue to purple, bell-shaped, and arranged in clusters, blooming in late summer to early autumn, and are pollinated by insects. The fruit is a follicle that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are thick, fleshy, and tuberous, adapted for storage and survival in various soil conditions.
