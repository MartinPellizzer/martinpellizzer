Aechmea koesteri

Aechmea koesteri, commonly known as the Koester's bromeliad, is a flowering plant native to South America, particularly Brazil.

It belongs to the Bromeliaceae family and is characterized by its rosette-shaped leaves that form a central cup to collect water. The plant produces striking inflorescences with vibrant red or orange flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette. Its foliage is often covered in a fine, white powdery coating called trichomes, which help in water absorption and protection against intense sunlight.

Aechmea koesteri is a popular ornamental plant due to its aesthetic appeal and relatively low maintenance requirements.

Morphology

Aechmea koesteri has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, strap-like, and often have a waxy surface, arranged in a tight spiral around the central rosette.

The leaves are typically green with a slight bluish tint and can grow up to 30 cm in length. The plant has a short, thick stem that is covered in a fibrous, rough texture and remains largely hidden beneath the leaves. Its flowers are borne in a spike-like inflorescence, consisting of bright red bracts with small white flowers inside, blooming during the summer months and pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds.

The fruit is a small, capsule-like structure that contains numerous seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.
