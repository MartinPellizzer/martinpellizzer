Aechmea mucroniflora

Aechmea mucroniflora, commonly known as the sharp-pointed bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its rosette of narrow, grass-like leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients. The plant produces vibrant, long-lasting inflorescences with bright red or orange flowers, often surrounded by colorful bracts. Its foliage is often covered in a waxy coating that aids in water retention.

Aechmea mucroniflora thrives in humid, shaded environments and is popular as a decorative houseplant.

Morphology

Aechmea mucroniflora has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, stiff, and covered with fine trichomes, arranged in a compact circle around the central stem.

The leaves are typically blue-green in color, with a waxy coating, and can grow up to 30 cm in length. The plant has a short, unbranched stem that is covered with a dense, fibrous bark, and it grows upright with a compact form. Its flowers are arranged in a dense inflorescence, with bright red bracts that enclose small white to pink flowers, blooming in the summer months and pollinated by birds, particularly hummingbirds.

The fruit is a fleshy capsule that disperses seeds by wind and animals, and the roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring in rocky or dry soil.
