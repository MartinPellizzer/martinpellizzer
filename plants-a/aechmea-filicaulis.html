Aechmea filicaulis

Aechmea filicaulis, commonly known as the silver pine bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the tropical regions of South America.

It is characterized by its tall, narrow leaves that form a rosette, creating a central cup that collects rainwater. The plant's foliage is often covered in a dense layer of white or silver hairs, giving it its common name. It produces vibrant red or orange flowers that bloom from the center of the rosette.

Aechmea filicaulis thrives in bright, indirect light and requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot.

Morphology

Aechmea filicaulis has rosette-shaped leaves that are sword-like, stiff, and arranged in a tight spiral, with a dark green color and a waxy coating.

The plant lacks a traditional stem, instead having a short, thick, and often hidden stem that supports the inflorescence. Its flowers are clustered in a spadix surrounded by a colorful bract, typically in shades of red or orange, and bloom during the summer months, relying on wind for pollination. The fruit is a capsule that contains small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in its bromeliad-like rosette, absorbing moisture from the air and rain.
