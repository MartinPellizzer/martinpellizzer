Aegilops bicornis

Aegilops bicornis, commonly known as the two-awned goatgrass, is a wild annual grass species native to the Mediterranean region.

It is characterized by its slender, erect stems and narrow, blade-like leaves. The plant produces small, spike-like inflorescences with two awns on each lemma, giving it its common name. Aegilops bicornis is often found in disturbed soils and can thrive in dry, temperate climates.

It has been studied for its potential in agricultural research due to its genetic relationship with wheat.

Morphology

Aegilops bicornis has narrow, grass-like leaves that are flat, parallel-veined, and typically arranged in a spiral pattern along the stem.

The stem is cylindrical, smooth, and slightly hairy, with a greenish-brown color, growing upright and branching sparingly. Its flowers are spike-like inflorescences with small, greenish-white florets, blooming in early summer, and are pollinated by wind. The fruit is a small, three-angled caryopsis, with seeds dispersed by wind due to their light weight.

The roots are fibrous and shallow, anchoring the plant in loose, sandy soils.
