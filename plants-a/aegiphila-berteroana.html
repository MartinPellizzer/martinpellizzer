Aegiphila berteroana

Aegiphila berteroana, commonly known as the Peruvian mountain mint, is a perennial shrub native to the Andean regions of South America.

It belongs to the Lamiaceae family, which is characterized by square stems and aromatic leaves. The plant typically grows in high-altitude environments, often in rocky or forested areas. Its leaves are lance-shaped and have a strong, mint-like fragrance.

It produces small, tubular flowers that are usually pale blue or purple, attracting various pollinators.

Morphology

Aegiphila berteroana has compound leaves that are pinnate, with small, ovate to lanceolate leaflets arranged alternately along the stem.

The leaves are typically green, with a slightly hairy texture, and vary in size from 2 to 5 cm in length. The stem is slender and erect, with a smooth, grayish-brown bark that becomes fissured with age. Its flowers are small, clustered in spikes, with white or pale pink petals, and bloom during the summer months; they are pollinated by insects such as bees.

The fruit is a dry, capsule that splits open to release small, wind-dispersed seeds.
