Aechmea martinicensis

Aechmea martinicensis, commonly known as the Martinique bromeliad, is a striking epiphytic plant native to the Caribbean.

It features a rosette of narrow, grass-like leaves that form a central cup, which collects water and nutrients. The plant's leaves are typically dark green with a metallic sheen and may have red or purple hues along the margins. It produces striking inflorescences with bright red or orange flowers that bloom in spring.

Aechmea martinicensis is well adapted to humid, shaded environments and is often found growing on trees or rocks in tropical forests.

Morphology

Aechmea martinicensis has rosette-shaped leaves that are broad, stiff, and sword-like, arranged in a tight spiral around the central rosette, with a bluish-green color and a waxy coating.

The plant lacks a true stem, with the leaves directly supporting the inflorescence, and the base of the leaves forms a dense, fibrous root system. Its inflorescence is a tall, branched spike bearing small, white to pale purple flowers with tubular petals and a showy bract, blooming during the dry season and pollinated by wind and insects. The fruit is a spiky, fleshy capsule that splits open to release small, black seeds, which are dispersed by wind and animals.

The roots are fibrous and adapted for anchoring the plant in rocky or sandy soils.
