Racinaea diffusa

Racinaea diffusa, commonly known as the ghost plant, is a unique aquatic plant characterized by its translucent, leaf-like structures that resemble ghostly shapes.

It thrives in slow-moving freshwater environments, often found in the understory of dense vegetation in wetlands or along the edges of ponds. The plant lacks true roots and instead absorbs nutrients through its leaf surfaces, which are covered in a thin, waxy layer. Its reproductive structures are small and inconspicuous, typically producing spores rather than seeds.

Due to its ethereal appearance and specialized adaptations, Racinaea diffusa is both ecologically significant and visually intriguing in its natural habitat.
