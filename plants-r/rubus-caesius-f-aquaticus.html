Rubus caesius f. aquaticus

Rubus caesius f. aquaticus, commonly known as the aquatic raspberry, is a variant of the common blackberry that thrives in wetland environments.

This plant is characterized by its trailing growth habit and its ability to form dense mats along the edges of ponds and streams. Its leaves are typically oval with serrated edges, and the flowers are small, pinkish-white, and clustered in the axils of the leaves. The fruit, which resembles a small blackberry, is edible and often used in local cuisines.

This subspecies is notable for its ecological role in stabilizing soil and providing habitat for aquatic and terrestrial wildlife.
