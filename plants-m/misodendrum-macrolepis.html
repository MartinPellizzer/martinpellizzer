Misodendrum macrolepis

Misodendrum macrolepis, commonly known as the broad-leaved mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that grows on a variety of host trees, primarily in tropical and subtropical regions.

It possesses specialized structures called haustoria that penetrate the host's vascular system to extract water and nutrients. The plant has alternate, ovate to elliptic leaves that are often covered in a waxy cuticle, which helps reduce water loss. Its small, white or yellowish flowers are typically clustered in inflorescences, and it produces small, fleshy berries that are a food source for various birds and mammals.

The species is notable for its ability to thrive in shaded environments and its role in the ecological dynamics of its host trees.
