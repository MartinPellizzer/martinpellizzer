Mistyllus turgidus

Mistyllus turgidus, commonly known as the swollen mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of host trees, primarily in the southern United States.

It possesses specialized structures called haustoria that penetrate the host's vascular system to extract water and nutrients. The plant produces small, white, fleshy berries that are a source of food for various birds and mammals. Its leaves are evergreen, thick, and waxy, which helps reduce water loss in arid environments.

Mistyllus turgidus plays a role in ecological succession by contributing to nutrient cycling and providing habitat for wildlife.
