Mystacidium obversifolium

Mystacidium obversifolium, commonly known as the reverse-leaf mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that grows on various host trees in tropical and subtropical regions.

It has small, leathery leaves that are typically arranged in an opposite fashion, which is reflected in its scientific name. The plant produces small, white or pale green flowers, followed by fleshy, berry-like fruits that are often consumed by birds. Its roots form specialized structures called haustoria, which attach to the host plant and extract water and nutrients.

This species plays a role in local ecosystems by providing food for wildlife and contributing to the ecological balance of its habitat.
