Mystacidium gilpiniae

Mystacidium gilpiniae, commonly known as the Gilpin's mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that grows on the branches of specific host trees, primarily in tropical and subtropical regions.

It possesses specialized structures called haustoria that penetrate the host's vascular system to extract water and nutrients. The plant produces small, white or pale green berries that are often consumed by birds, which then disperse the seeds. It plays a role in ecological interactions by influencing host tree health and supporting avian populations.

Its botanical characteristics reflect adaptations to a parasitic lifestyle while maintaining some degree of photosynthetic capability.
