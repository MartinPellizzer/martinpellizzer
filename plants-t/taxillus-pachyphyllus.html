Taxillus pachyphyllus

Taxillus pachyphyllus, commonly known as the thick-leaf mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of its host trees, primarily in the genus Eucalyptus.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while also producing its own sugars through photosynthesis using the light it captures. The plant's thick, leathery leaves are adapted to reduce water loss in arid environments. It produces small, white or pale flowers that are often visited by insects for pollination.

The berries it bears are typically red or yellow and are consumed by birds, which aid in seed dispersal.
