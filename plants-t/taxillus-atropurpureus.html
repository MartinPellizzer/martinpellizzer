Taxillus atropurpureus

Taxillus atropurpureus, commonly known as the purple mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of various host trees, primarily oaks and maples.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while still performing photosynthesis using the limited chlorophyll it possesses. The plant produces small, fleshy berries that are typically red or purple in color, which contain seeds dispersed by birds. Its dense, evergreen foliage forms a compact, bushy growth that can cover significant portions of its host.

Taxillus atropurpureus plays a role in local ecosystems by providing food and habitat for certain wildlife species.
