Taxillus matsudae

Taxillus matsudae, commonly known as the parasitic mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of its host, typically members of the Eucalyptus genus.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while still performing some photosynthesis using modified leaves. The plant produces small, white or greenish flowers and red, fleshy berries that are often consumed by birds. Its life cycle involves seed dispersal by animals, and it plays a role in the ecological dynamics of its native habitats.

Taxillus matsudae is found primarily in Southeast Asia, including parts of Japan and Indonesia.
