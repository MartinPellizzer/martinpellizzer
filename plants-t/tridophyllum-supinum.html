Tridophyllum supinum

Tridophyllum supinum, commonly known as the parasitic mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches itself to the branches of its host trees, primarily in the genus Eucalyptus.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while still performing some photosynthesis using a limited amount of chlorophyll. Its small, scale-like leaves are adapted to reduce water loss, and it produces small, white or yellowish flowers that are often inconspicuous. The plant's fruit is a small, fleshy berry that contains seeds covered in a sticky substance to aid in attachment to new hosts.

Tridophyllum supinum plays a role in the ecological dynamics of its habitat, influencing host tree growth and providing food for certain bird species.
