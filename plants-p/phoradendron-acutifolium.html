Phoradendron acutifolium

Phoradendron acutifolium, commonly known as the mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of host trees, primarily in the southeastern United States.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while also producing its own sugars through photosynthesis using the sunlight it captures. The plant has small, scale-like leaves and produces white or yellowish berries that are consumed by birds, which then disperse the seeds. Its growth is slow, and it can live for several decades, forming dense clusters on its host.

The plant's unique relationship with its host and its role in local ecosystems make it an important component of forest biodiversity.
