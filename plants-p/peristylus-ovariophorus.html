Peristylus ovariophorus

Peristylus ovariophorus, commonly known as the long-fruited mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of its host trees, primarily in tropical and subtropical regions.

It possesses specialized structures called haustoria that penetrate the host's vascular system to extract water and nutrients. The plant produces small, white or pale green berries that are often dispersed by birds. Its leaves are usually opposite and have a leathery texture, while its flowers are unisexual, with separate male and female structures.

Peristylus ovariophorus plays a role in forest ecosystems by influencing host tree growth and providing food for certain wildlife species.
