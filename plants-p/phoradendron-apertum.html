Phoradendron apertum

Phoradendron apertum, commonly known as the common mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of host trees, primarily in the Americas.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while producing its own sugars through photosynthesis using sunlight. The plant's berries are often white or yellow and are a food source for birds, which then disperse the seeds. Its sticky, gelatinous seeds adhere to the bark of host trees, facilitating propagation.

Phoradendron apertum plays a role in forest ecosystems by influencing host tree growth and providing resources for wildlife.
