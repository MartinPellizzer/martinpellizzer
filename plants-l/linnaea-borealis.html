Linnaea borealis

Linnaea borealis, commonly known as the twinflower, is a small evergreen shrub native to the northern regions of North America and Eurasia.

It is characterized by its distinctive pairs of opposite, oval-shaped leaves and its delicate, fragrant flowers that bloom in early spring. The plant produces small, red berries that are an important food source for various bird species. Linnaea borealis thrives in shaded, moist environments and is often found in boreal and temperate forests.

Its scientific name honors Carl Linnaeus, the father of modern taxonomy, who first described the species.
