Loranthus laurinus

Loranthus laurinus, commonly known as the laurel mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of its host trees, primarily oaks and elms.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while producing its own carbohydrates through photosynthesis using light reflected from the host's leaves. The plant produces small, white or greenish flowers and red berries that are an important food source for birds and other wildlife. It is often found in temperate regions and is considered an indicator of healthy forest ecosystems.

Its presence can affect the growth and health of its host tree, making it both ecologically significant and sometimes problematic.
