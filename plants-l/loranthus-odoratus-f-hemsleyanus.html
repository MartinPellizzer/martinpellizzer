Loranthus odoratus f. hemsleyanus

Loranthus odoratus f. hemsleyanus, commonly known as the fragrant mistletoe, is a hemiparasitic plant that attaches to the branches of its host trees, primarily in tropical and subtropical regions.

It lacks chlorophyll and relies on its host for water and nutrients, while producing its own sugars through photosynthesis using the sunlight that reaches its leaves. The plant is characterized by its small, lance-shaped leaves and clusters of small, white or pale green flowers that bloom during the rainy season. Its fruit is a capsule that releases tiny seeds, which can adhere to the bark of host trees, facilitating further propagation.

This species is often cultivated for its ornamental value and is also used in traditional medicine for its purported healing properties.
